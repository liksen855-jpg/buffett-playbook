The Dip-Buy Thesis

Selling the fear,
not the business.

Three high-quality companies are sitting near lows — not because their numbers broke, but because the market believed a story. This note walks from the macro backdrop down to the three names, and the dated catalyst that forces each one to re-rate.

META  Meta Platforms NOW  ServiceNow UBER  Uber Technologies
I

The backdropDemand is accelerating. Prices aren't.

Start with the thing nobody disputes: the AI build-out is not slowing down. Heading into 2026, Microsoft, Meta and Amazon each raised their infrastructure budgets rather than trimming them — Meta alone guided to $125–145 billion in capital spending for the year. The end-demand for compute is the strongest it has ever been.

The equity reaction, however, has been wildly uneven. Capital rotated toward the biggest turnaround narratives — Intel ran roughly 250% year-to-date on a recovery story — while the bellwether that actually sells the picks and shovels barely moved. The market, right now, is not paying for the best business. It is paying for the widest gap between perception and reality.

II

The dislocationWhere the fear is, the value follows.

That same mechanic has stranded three durable compounders near multi-year lows. In each case the sell-off traces to a narrative — AI spending that will never pay off, automation that eats the seats, robotaxis that end the network — rather than to any deterioration in the underlying results.

The setup is the same across all three. Buy the business the crowd is pricing as broken, against a floor that sits above today's price, ahead of a dated catalyst that forces the re-rate. What follows is the case for each, from the story the market is telling to the math it has stopped doing.

The narrative

Everyone is panicking about the AI bill. But Meta is the medium — roughly 3.5 billion daily users and an advertising engine that AI plugs straight into. This is not AI as a cost centre hunting for a use case; it is AI that is revenue-accretive on day one, because better targeting and ranking is the product. A corkscrew that can't be pulled out of the system.

The catalyst

Q2 earnings on 29 July. One question carries the print: does the spend start showing up in ad pricing? The moment capital expenditure converts to monetisation, the label flips from reckless spender to cheapest compounder in large-cap tech.

The asymmetry

The floor is the distribution moat — survival isn't the question — so you underwrite a near-certain base and get the AI optionality cheap. At ~17× forward earnings, a mega-cap on ~40% operating margins is on the clearance rack, with expectations washed out enough that one good print re-rates it hard. A ten-method composite puts fair value at $669.77 against a $550.25 tape — call it 22% upside on a trimmed-mean basis, with the DCF-driven methods running considerably higher.

"I don't see a world without Meta. Everyone's scared of the AI bill — but Meta is the rails AI runs on."

The narrative

There is controversy here, and the stock has been punished for it. But ServiceNow quietly became the AI control tower for the Fortune 500 — more than 85% of them run on it. And the validators are lining up: an expanded Nvidia partnership, an IBM watsonx alliance, and Trump disclosed as a buyer. When that many names are involved, the question stops being if.

The catalyst

Q2 earnings on 22 July. The line to watch: does AI product revenue accelerate faster than the business loses seats to automation? Management already lifted its AI revenue target to $1.5 billion. A beat plus a raise to organic guidance — held flat last quarter, to the market's disappointment — flips the chart.

The asymmetry

An oversold name near a 52-week low, an AI inflection, and a single catalyst is the recipe for a sharp snap-back. Across the shocks ServiceNow has fully recovered from, the median time back to a pre-shock high has been roughly three months — and you're buying a structural winner mid-washout. The composite model puts fair value at $144.74 against a $98.34 tape, about 47% upside on a trimmed-mean basis — though note three of ten methods (DNI-20, EV/Revenue, Graham Revised) come in below today's price, a reminder this name is more split-decision than the other two.

"When Nvidia, IBM and Trump are all in, a 52-week low doesn't last."

What breaks it
Robotaxis compress take-rates, or AV partners go direct; bookings decelerate below guide.

The narrative

Uber sits near a 52-week low because the market thinks robotaxis kill it. The data says the opposite: where Waymo runs on Uber's app, trips per vehicle per day are up 30%, and those have become some of Uber's fastest-growing markets. The crowd is pricing Uber as the victim of autonomy when the evidence says it's the aggregator — the platform autonomy needs to reach riders.

The catalyst

Q2 earnings in early August — gross bookings against the guided 18–22% — on top of a stacking partnership narrative and the Uber Eats retail expansion. And the smart-money tell: a disclosed $500K–$1M position in deep in-the-money Uber calls expiring March 2027, a measured leveraged-long that lines up with this exact window.

The asymmetry

The floor sits well above $76. Uber compounds bookings at 20–25% and EBITDA near 33%, trades on a ~6.5% free-cash-flow yield, and is priced as if the disruption is already fact. If autonomy proves additive rather than dilutive, the re-rate is large — and even the conservative H-Model on the page ($121.34, +59%) implies meaningful upside. The ten-method composite lands at $244.89, more than triple the tape — though that figure leans on the growth-extrapolation methods; Wall Street's own consensus target sits closer to $105, so treat $244.89 as the model's optimistic case, not the Street's.

"Where the robotaxis already run on Uber's app, rides per car jumped thirty percent."
