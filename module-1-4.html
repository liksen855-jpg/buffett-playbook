Inflation &
Purchasing Power

How inflation is measured, what drives it, and the four macro quadrant framework that separates retail investors from the professionals who get paid to position for it. Inflation is not a threat to avoid — it is a regime to map and exploit.

70 min Article · 2 Tools · 10-Q Quiz
Section 01

What Is Inflation?

Inflation is the rate at which the general level of prices rises over time — equivalently, the rate at which the purchasing power of money declines. It is not one price rising; it is the broad price level shifting upward across an economy.

The commonly cited measure is the Consumer Price Index (CPI), which tracks a basket of goods and services representing typical household spending. But CPI is only one measure — and arguably the most politically managed one. Investors need to know all four:

MeasureWhat It TracksKey LimitationInvestor Use
CPIConsumer goods basketExcludes volatile food/energy (Core CPI); subject to methodological changesMost widely quoted; market reaction benchmark
PPIProducer/wholesale pricesLeads CPI by 1–3 months; not a direct consumer measureBest forward signal for where CPI is going
PCE DeflatorPersonal consumption expenditureFed's preferred measure — harder to follow; different basket weightsWhat the Fed actually targets for policy decisions
Breakeven RateBond market inflation expectation (TIPS spread)Market-implied, reflects sentiment as much as fundamentalsBest real-time forward indicator — money is on the line
The Professional Edge

The inflation breakeven rate — derived from the yield gap between nominal Treasury yields and TIPS (inflation-protected bonds) — is the market's real-time forecast of future inflation. It is more predictive than any government survey because it represents actual capital at risk. When the 5yr/5yr forward breakeven moves, institutional desks reprice every rate-sensitive asset within minutes. Watch it weekly.

Section 02

The Three Drivers of Inflation

Inflation is not a single phenomenon — it has distinct causes that require different policy responses and create different investment opportunities. Knowing which type of inflation you are in is as important as knowing the inflation rate itself.

Inflation Causation Framework

Why the Driver Matters for Your Portfolio

Demand-pull inflation responds to tighter monetary policy — rate hikes reduce spending and cool the economy. Cost-push inflation from supply shocks is much harder to fix with rates. Raising rates does not create more oil or fix supply chains — it just kills demand, which means economic contraction without necessarily solving the inflation. Built-in (expectations-driven) inflation is the most dangerous because it is self-reinforcing, requiring the central bank to break expectations by inflicting severe economic pain — exactly what Volcker did in 1979–82 with rates reaching 20%.

Asymmetric Edge — Diagnosing the Type

The inflation type determines which assets win. Demand-pull inflation (too much money) = central bank will tighten aggressively = position for rate hikes (short bonds, overweight financials, reduce growth stock duration). Cost-push inflation from an oil shock = monetary tightening won't fix supply = buy the commodity producers causing the inflation (the Iran/oil thesis, the rubber comeback thesis — these are cost-push trades). Built-in inflation (wage-price spiral locked in) = long multi-year inflation = buy commodity royalties, real estate, infrastructure — assets with pricing power that compound over a decade. The same "inflation" word covers three completely different investment theses.

Section 03

How Inflation Distorts Investment Decisions

Inflation does not affect all investments equally. Understanding the inflation sensitivity of different asset classes is fundamental to portfolio construction — and the hierarchy is not intuitive.

The Hyperinflation Lesson

Germany's Weimar Republic (1921–23) saw inflation peak at 29,500% per month. Zimbabwe in 2008 reached 89.7 sextillion percent annually. In both cases, real assets — land, gold, foreign currency — survived. Cash and government bonds became worthless. These are extreme cases — but they demonstrate the fundamental principle at any inflation level: in inflation, real assets preserve, paper assets erode.

Section 04 · Case Study

The 2021–2023 Inflation Cycle

The post-COVID inflation episode was the most significant inflationary event in 40 years — and a textbook example of all three inflation drivers operating simultaneously, which is exactly what made it so difficult to defeat.

  • Demand-pull component: $5+ trillion in US fiscal stimulus (CARES Act, ARP) flooded the economy with cash while supply was constrained. Demand surged; supply could not respond. This was inflation by design.
  • Cost-push component: Global supply chain disruption, semiconductor shortages, shipping bottlenecks — then the 2022 Ukraine-Russia war driving energy and food prices sharply higher. These are structural supply shocks that rate hikes cannot directly fix.
  • Built-in component: Labour markets tightened (the Great Resignation), workers demanded higher wages, firms passed costs to consumers. Shelter CPI — which has an 18-month lag because rents reset annually — became the stickiest driver and the hardest to kill.

The Fed's response: 525 basis points of rate hikes in 16 months — the fastest tightening cycle since Volcker. CPI peaked at 9.1% in June 2022 and declined toward 3% by 2024. The lag between monetary tightening and inflation response is typically 12–18 months, which is why central banks must act before inflation becomes entrenched — and why they invariably overshoot on both sides.

The Trade Thesis Connection

Understanding the inflation cycle creates tradeable insights. In 2021, recognising that demand-pull inflation was building ahead of Fed action meant positioning in commodities, energy stocks, and real assets before the institutional herd caught on. In early 2022, anticipating aggressive rate hikes meant avoiding long-duration bonds and high-multiple growth stocks. The same inflation framework that looks like dry economics in a textbook is live market alpha when applied in real time.

Section 05 · Asymmetric Framework

Dalio's Four Macro Quadrants: The Complete Portfolio Map

The single most important upgrade to a standard inflation curriculum. Ray Dalio's All-Weather framework maps every economic environment on two axes: the direction of growth (rising or falling) and the direction of inflation (rising or falling). The intersection produces four quadrants — and each quadrant has a distinct set of asset class winners and losers based on 100 years of evidence.

This is not theoretical. Dalio built Bridgewater's All-Weather fund explicitly to hold exposure across all four quadrants simultaneously — so that portfolio performance is never dependent on correctly predicting which regime is coming. Most retail investors are unknowingly concentrated in a single quadrant. When it ends, so does their outperformance.

"
There are only a few things that move markets: growth and inflation. Everything else is noise. If you understand those two variables, you can position for almost anything that happens.
— Ray Dalio, Bridgewater Associates
Q1
↑ Growth   ↑ Inflation
Inflationary Boom
Economy growing, inflation rising. Central bank is behind the curve or deliberately accommodating. Credit expanding. Nominal assets rise but real returns compressed by inflation. Late-cycle environment. The 2021 playbook.
✓ Commodities · Energy stocks · Banks · Emerging markets · Inflation-linked bonds (TIPS) · Real estate
✗ Long-duration bonds · Cash · Fixed income broadly
Q2
↑ Growth   ↓ Inflation
Goldilocks
The ideal investment environment. Growing economy, contained inflation, accommodative or neutral central bank. Equity bull markets are born and sustained here. 2013–2019, the post-GFC decade, fits this template almost perfectly.
✓ Growth equities · Technology · Credit / high yield · Real estate · Broad equity exposure
✗ Gold (relative) · Commodities · Defensive equities
Q3
↓ Growth   ↑ Inflation
Stagflation — The Worst
The most destructive macro regime for traditional portfolios. Growth is slowing or negative while inflation remains elevated. The central bank is trapped — it cannot cut (inflation too high) or hike further (growth too weak). There is no good policy option. The 1970s US playbook. Both stocks and bonds fall.
✓ Gold · Oil & hard commodities · Commodity producers · TIPS · Short nominal bonds
✗ Almost everything else — equities, long bonds, credit, real estate
Q4
↓ Growth   ↓ Inflation
Deflationary Contraction
Growth falling, prices falling. The central bank's nightmare — deflation makes debt burdens rise in real terms, which can trigger self-reinforcing decline. Policy response: aggressive money printing (QE). The 2008–2009 template. Short-lived by design — central banks always fight this with maximum force.
✓ Cash (temporarily) · Long-duration government bonds · Gold · Short equities (briefly)
✗ Equities · Credit · Real estate · Commodities
Asymmetric Edge — Mapping Your Current Quadrant

The two-variable diagnostic: Every week, ask two questions: Is economic growth accelerating or decelerating? Is inflation running above or below 2%? The intersection maps directly to a quadrant. Your portfolio should be sized toward the quadrant you're in — and beginning to position for the quadrant you're transitioning to.

The transition is where the money is made: Q2 (Goldilocks) → Q1 (Inflationary Boom): start buying commodities and energy before the consensus sees inflation building. Q1 → Q3 (Stagflation): the critical move — sell equities broadly, concentrate in gold and commodity producers. Q3 → Q4 (Deflation): the pivot — buy long-duration bonds before the Fed cuts. Q4 → Q2: the recovery trade — buy equities aggressively during the depths of the deflationary bust, before growth resumes.

Malaysian lens: In 2022–23, Malaysia was in Q1 transitioning to Q3 — fuel subsidy pressures keeping inflation structurally elevated while global growth slowed. PCHEM's surge with oil prices, the ringgit weakness, and BNM's cautious tightening all map precisely onto this quadrant framework.

Section 06 · Asymmetric Framework

Leading vs. Lagging Inflation Indicators

The CPI print that makes headlines is a lagging indicator — it tells you what inflation was 1–3 months ago. By the time the consensus is reacting to a high CPI print, the investment opportunity is already partially priced in. Professionals watch the indicators that predict where CPI will be in 3–6 months.

ISM Prices Paid
2–3 MONTHS LEADING
The Institute for Supply Management's survey of manufacturer input cost pressure. When Prices Paid is above 60 and rising, CPI will follow within 2–3 months. It peaked at 92.1 in June 2021 — 12 months before CPI hit 9.1%. Professionals were positioned in commodities and energy while retail was still confused about "transitory."
5yr/5yr Forward Breakeven
REAL-TIME FORWARD
The bond market's estimate of average inflation between years 5 and 10 from now. This is the Fed's actual target anchor. When this rate surges above 2.5%, the Fed will tighten — virtually without exception. When it falls below 1.5%, the Fed will ease. Watch it weekly on the St. Louis Fed FRED database.
PPI (Producer Prices)
1–3 MONTHS LEADING
Wholesale prices at the factory gate — before they reach the consumer. When producers pay more for inputs, they eventually pass it on. PPI leads CPI by 1–3 months. Core PPI (excluding food and energy) is particularly useful for identifying durable pricing pressure vs. transitory commodity shocks.
Shelter CPI
6–18 MONTHS LAGGING
The CPI's shelter component (35% of the index) is the most lagged. It measures rents based on lease renewals, which reset annually. Private market rent data (Zillow, Apartment List) showed rents falling from late 2022 — but this didn't appear in official CPI until 18 months later. Understanding this lag prevents wrong conclusions about "sticky" inflation.
Average Hourly Earnings
CONCURRENT / LAGGING
Wage growth drives built-in inflation. When average hourly earnings run consistently above 4%, it signals wage-price spiral risk. The Fed watches the 3-month annualised rate closely. Above 5% for multiple months = tightening without hesitation. The 2022–23 cycle kept wage growth elevated even as goods inflation fell — this is what made the "last mile" of disinflation so difficult.
Commodity Spot Prices
1–6 MONTHS LEADING
Crude oil prices lead headline CPI by 1–2 months (gasoline price changes are immediate). Copper leads industrial goods inflation. Agricultural commodity prices (wheat, corn, soy) lead food CPI. A sustained 30%+ rise in oil almost always translates to a 1–2% CPI uplift within 2 quarters. Watch the Bloomberg Commodity Index for the broadest signal.
Asymmetric Edge — The Leading Indicator Dashboard

Build a simple monthly checklist: ISM Prices Paid direction (up or down?), forward breakevens (above or below 2.5%?), PPI trend (accelerating or decelerating?), oil price 3-month change, wage growth 3-month annualised rate. If 4 or 5 of these point in the same direction, you are 2–3 months ahead of the consensus CPI print. That is where the asymmetric trade lives — before the headline number confirms what the leading indicators already told you.

Section 07 · The Danger Zone

Stagflation: The Portfolio Killer Most Investors Are Unprepared For

Stagflation is the least understood and most destructive macro regime for a traditional portfolio. It happens infrequently enough that most investors have never experienced it — but understanding it in advance is the difference between preserving wealth and losing 40–60% in real terms.

"
In stagflation, almost everything goes down together. The only hedge is to have been right before it started — to have built the position when it was still uncomfortable and early.
— Howard Marks, Oaktree Capital

Why Traditional Portfolios Have No Defence

The standard 60/40 portfolio (60% equities, 40% bonds) is built for the Goldilocks quadrant. In stagflation, it fails completely:

  • Equities fail because slowing growth compresses earnings while rising inflation compresses multiple valuations simultaneously. Double compression. The S&P fell −47% in real terms during the 1973–74 stagflationary episode.
  • Bonds fail because the central bank cannot cut rates to support bond prices — inflation is too high. Long-duration bonds are effectively trapped: too much inflation to allow easing, too much economic weakness to sustain high rates indefinitely.
  • Cash fails because its real value is destroyed by the inflation component of stagflation.
  • Only hard assets survive: oil, gold, agricultural land, commodity producers, royalty streams. These are the assets that win in stagflation — not because they are great investments in absolute terms, but because everything else is worse.

The 1970s Case Study — The Playbook

The 1973–1980 stagflationary decade in the US produced the definitive playbook. The triggers: OPEC oil embargo (cost-push), Nixon's fiscal expansion (demand-pull), and wage-price controls that entrenched expectations. What happened:

  • Oil companies: Exxon, Chevron rose 300–500% in the decade while the S&P fell in real terms
  • Gold: Rose from $35/oz (when Nixon ended Bretton Woods in 1971) to $850/oz by 1980 — a 24x nominal gain
  • Agricultural land: Surged as food prices spiked globally
  • Equities broadly: Lost 47% of real value 1973–74, then recovered nominally but barely kept pace with inflation through the decade
  • Long-duration bonds: Catastrophic — 30-year yields rose from 6% to 15%, implying ~50% price decline
Asymmetric Edge — Positioning Before Stagflation Arrives

The stagflation trade must be built early — when the environment still looks like normal inflation and the consensus still believes central banks can engineer a soft landing. By the time stagflation is confirmed, commodity prices have already run 50–100% and the easy money is gone.

The early warning checklist: (1) oil prices rising sharply from a supply shock (not demand), (2) core inflation remaining above 4% despite rate hikes, (3) GDP growth forecasts being revised down by 1%+ while inflation forecasts remain elevated, (4) the Fed's language shifting from "we can control inflation" to "we expect a challenging period." When three of four are present, the stagflation rotation — into gold, energy producers, agricultural commodities, short nominal duration — is the asymmetric trade.
