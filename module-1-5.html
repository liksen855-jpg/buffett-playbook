Section 01

The Three Financial Statements

Every public company is required to publish three financial statements. Together, they tell the complete story of a business — what it earned, what it owns, and how cash moves through it. Read any one in isolation and you will be misled. Read all three together and you see the business clearly.

The Three Statements & How They Connect
Section 02

The Income Statement: Measuring Profitability

The income statement (also called P&L — profit and loss) measures a company's revenue and expenses over a period, culminating in net income. But net income is not the same as cash. This distinction is crucial.

The Key Metrics Investors Actually Use

  • Gross Margin: (Revenue − COGS) ÷ Revenue. Measures how efficiently the core product is produced. A software company at 70%+ gross margin vs a retailer at 25% tells you entirely different things about the business model.
  • Operating Margin (EBIT margin): Operating profit ÷ Revenue. Shows profitability after all operational costs but before financing and tax. Useful for comparing companies across capital structures.
  • EBITDA: Earnings before interest, tax, depreciation, amortization. Widely used as a proxy for cash earnings — though aggressively abused by companies wanting to show better "adjusted" results.
  • Net Margin: Net income ÷ Revenue. The bottom line after everything. Wide variation by industry (tech: 20–30%, retail: 2–5%, banking: 10–15%).
What Analysts Actually Watch

Revenue growth rate, gross margin trend (expanding or compressing?), and operating leverage (does revenue growth convert to disproportionate operating profit growth?). A company growing revenue at 20% with expanding margins and operating leverage is a fundamentally different proposition than one growing at 20% with margin compression.

Section 03

The Balance Sheet: Measuring Financial Health

The balance sheet is a snapshot of what a company owns (assets) and owes (liabilities) at a specific date. The difference is shareholders' equity — the net worth of the business from an accounting perspective.

The fundamental equation: Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity. This must always balance — hence the name.

What Investors Look For

  • Cash position: How much runway does the company have? Is cash growing or shrinking? Cash is optionality — Berkshire Hathaway's cash hoard is a strategic weapon, not a failure to deploy.
  • Debt-to-equity ratio: How leveraged is the business? High debt amplifies returns in good times and accelerates distress in bad ones. Capital-intensive businesses (airlines, utilities) naturally carry more debt than asset-light tech companies.
  • Working capital: Current assets minus current liabilities. Negative working capital can signal liquidity stress — or, in the case of Amazon, that suppliers essentially finance the business.
  • Goodwill and intangibles: Large goodwill balances from acquisitions warrant scrutiny — they represent what the acquirer paid above book value. Goodwill impairments are often the first sign of a bad acquisition.

Section 04

The Cash Flow Statement: The Truth Teller

If the income statement can be massaged with accounting choices, the cash flow statement is harder to fake. Cash either arrived in the bank account or it did not. This is why experienced investors weight the cash flow statement most heavily.

Free Cash Flow — The Most Important Number

Free Cash Flow Formula
FCF = Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditure

FCF is what remains after maintaining and growing the business — available for dividends, buybacks, debt repayment, or reinvestment

A company can report strong net income while generating negative free cash flow — a red flag that often precedes financial distress. Conversely, companies with modest net income but strong free cash flow conversion are often undervalued by earnings-focused analysts.

FCF Yield: The Investor's Filter

FCF Yield = Free Cash Flow ÷ Market Capitalization. This tells you the return you are getting in actual cash terms for every dollar of market value. A company with 8% FCF yield is generating 8 cents of real cash for every dollar of its market cap — a useful sanity check against headline earnings multiples.

Reading the Three Sections

  • Operating activities (CFO): Cash generated from the core business. This should consistently exceed net income in a healthy, cash-generative business. If CFO < Net income, ask why.
  • Investing activities (CFI): Cash spent on CapEx, acquisitions, and investments. Negative is normal — it means the company is investing in growth. Excessive CapEx relative to depreciation may signal overcapitalization.
  • Financing activities (CFF): Cash flows from raising/repaying debt, issuing/buying back shares, paying dividends. Net borrowing during downturns is a red flag; share buybacks at peak valuations destroy capital.
