01 — The Setup

What just happened — and why it matters to your money

On February 28, 2026, Iranian Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei was killed in a joint U.S.-Israeli military operation codenamed Operation Epic Fury. Multiple senior Iranian military officials died in simultaneous strikes. The Islamic Republic, which has run Iran for 46 years, is now in a leadership vacuum with no historical precedent.

Every news outlet is telling you the same thing: oil goes up, buy defense stocks, fill your tank. They're not wrong about the direction. They're wrong about the certainty. The situation you're actually looking at is far more complex — and far more interesting.

The same conflict, depending entirely on how the next 30 days unfold, produces radically opposite outcomes for energy markets. In one scenario, $100 to $150 a barrel and a potential global recession. In the other, a price collapse toward $50 and the largest expansion of oil supply since the U.S. shale revolution.

"The market is currently pricing Scenario A. It is not pricing Scenario B at all. That gap is the trade."

The core thesis

Standard financial coverage is only telling you about one of these outcomes. This breakdown covers both — and, more importantly, what to do about the uncertainty between them.

02 — The Geography

The Strait of Hormuz:
Why 33 kilometers rules the world

Before the economics make sense, you need to understand one piece of geography that controls more of the global economy than almost any other physical location on earth.

The Strait of Hormuz is a strip of water — 33 kilometres wide at its narrowest point — that sits between Iran to the north and Oman to the south. Iran's coastline directly borders it. Every day, an enormous volume of the world's oil exits through this single chokepoint.

0 million barrels of oil pass through per day
20% of everything the world burns in oil daily
27% of global seaborne crude oil trade
22% of global LNG supply — mostly Qatari
33km narrowest width of the strait — Iran's coast borders it

Saudi Arabia's oil. Iraq's oil. Kuwait, UAE, Qatar — all of it exits through Hormuz. The pipeline alternatives that bypass it are limited. Saudi Arabia has a Red Sea pipeline; the UAE has a Fujairah pipeline. Together, they can reroute about 2.6 million barrels per day. Against 20 million at risk: that's 13%.

The West talks about Hormuz as an American problem. The numbers show it's primarily an Asian problem — which creates its own set of geopolitical complications, especially regarding China's response to any disruption.

03 — The Paradox

Two extreme outcomes.
One conflict.

Here is the core of what no one is explaining. Both of these scenarios are real possibilities from the same event. The investment implications are completely opposite.

Scenario A — Retaliation
$150
Trigger: Iran closes or disrupts Hormuz

Iran's IRGC — even in a leaderless state — has the military capability to mine Hormuz, fire on tankers, or make the strait too dangerous for commercial transit. This requires no central command decision. Local commanders can act.

  • 20 million barrels per day disappears from global supply overnight
  • Spare capacity is trapped behind the blockade — it can't reach markets
  • The Strategic Petroleum Reserve releases ~1M bbl/day — covers 5% of the gap
  • Pipeline alternatives cover 13% at best
  • "Mother of all bidding wars" — prices must rise until demand collapses
  • China faces energy crisis. Europe faces LNG squeeze. Global recession risk rises sharply

Analyst consensus: Lombard Odier estimates $100+ per barrel. Kpler and CNBC sources suggest $150 is plausible in a full closure scenario. One analyst quoted directly: "A prolonged closure is a guaranteed global recession."

Scenario B — Regime Change
$50
Trigger: Regime falls, sanctions lift

If the regime collapses and a transitional government emerges — whether through popular uprising, IRGC negotiation, or U.S.-backed installation — sanctions get lifted. This is the scenario that zero mainstream financial media is currently modelling.

  • Iran's pre-1979 production capacity: 6 million barrels/day
  • Current output: ~3 million barrels/day (capped by 45 years of sanctions and underinvestment)
  • Realistic post-sanctions potential: 3.8M+ barrels/day within 12–18 months
  • Net addition to global supply: ~1.9 million new barrels per day
  • The world was already oversupplied before this conflict began
  • Western oil majors re-enter: Chevron, Shell, TotalEnergies bring capital and technology

For context: 1.9 million barrels is larger than Kuwait's entire production. It's more than the output gain that crashed oil prices in 2014 when U.S. shale ramped up. In an oversupplied market, this is deflationary for energy prices.

"Options markets cannot properly price a bimodal outcome of this magnitude. That's not a problem. That's an opportunity."

The sophisticated response to a bimodal distribution isn't to pick a side. It's to either position for maximum uncertainty — assets that pay off in both scenarios — or to construct a straddle where you capture upside from whichever extreme materialises.

06 — The Hidden Variable

The story mainstream
media isn't covering

Beyond the oil price question, there is one economic variable in this situation that is almost entirely absent from financial media coverage. It is potentially the most significant wealth-transfer event connected to this conflict.

The IRGC controls an estimated 30–40% of Iran's formal GDP. It runs the country's largest mobile operator (55M+ subscribers), its biggest construction conglomerate, significant oil infrastructure, and a network of financial institutions. It is not just a military — it is a state within a state, with an economic empire estimated at $400 billion or more. And as of this week, it has no supreme leader. For the first time in 46 years.

What happens to that empire — whether it gets privatised in a rapid transition, fragmented in prolonged conflict, or consolidated by the Guard itself — is the question that will define who captures the most value from post-war Iran. It is the subject of Video 2 in this series.

Separately: China's position is catastrophically underreported. China receives 80%+ of Iran's oil exports at a 15-25% discount to market price — a discount that exists only because sanctions made Iran desperate to sell to whoever would buy. A regime change scenario eliminates this discount overnight. China also has a $400 billion, 25-year Belt and Road cooperation deal with Iran that is now in jeopardy. This is the subject of Video 3.
