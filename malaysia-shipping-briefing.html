Two chokepoints, one war: the structural case for Malaysian container ports

Key takeaways

This is not a buy signal. It's a structural idea worth exploring, with the mechanism, the data, and the risks laid out for you to judge.

  • Two chokepoints, one strategy. Iran fights directly at the Strait of Hormuz — its own coastline — while using the Houthis as a proxy to threaten the separate Bab el-Mandeb / Red Sea corridor.
  • The reroute is structural, not cyclical. Cape of Good Hope oil traffic rose from 6.2 to 9.1 million barrels/day between 2023 and 1H 2025, a gain that has held for two years.
  • Malaysia captured the overflow by default. Deep water and spare berth capacity — not policy — routed Singapore's 2024 congestion into Port Klang and Tanjung Pelepas.
  • Westports Holdings (WPRTS MK) is the listed pure-play on Port Klang's largest terminal; analysts explicitly flag a Suez reopening as a risk to its own volume guidance.
  • The thesis has a clear exit. Q1 2026 throughput at Westports actually declined 1.1% y/y; the war has not yet shown up as a volume tailwind.
SECTION 1

Situation overview — one war, two chokepoints

Since the US-Iran war began on 28 February 2026, Iran has engaged directly at the Strait of Hormuz — a partial blockade and repeated strikes on shipping have disrupted the majority of Gulf oil and LNG exports through a waterway that normally carries approximately 20.9 million barrels per day, roughly a quarter of global seaborne oil trade.

A second, lower-cost lever exists. The Houthi movement in Yemen, armed and aligned with Iran through its "Axis of Resistance," controls territory overlooking Bab el-Mandeb, the entrance to the Red Sea. Iranian officials this week reportedly requested the Houthis close the strait in the event of US strikes on Iranian energy infrastructure, with Houthi forces already positioned to act.

SECTION 2

Transmission mechanism

This is not a hypothetical chain — it already occurred once, independent of the current war. When Houthi attacks made the Red Sea unsafe from late 2023, container carriers abandoned the Suez Canal almost entirely; by Q4 2025 container transits sat 86% below 2023 levels. Vessels rerouted via the Cape of Good Hope, adding 10 to 14 days per voyage.

The schedule disruption broke Asia's hub network. Singapore, the region's default transshipment hub, saw berth waits extend to two to three days in 2024 as carriers skipped planned calls. Reuters and Linerlytica both identified the same overflow destinations at the time: Port Klang and Tanjung Pelepas, Malaysia.

Analyst note

The Red Sea crisis did not divert cargo to Malaysia directly. It broke the region's routing map, and Malaysia's spare capacity happened to sit where the redrawn lines landed.

SECTION 3

Official EIA chokepoint accounting shows the redirected volume cleanly: barrels that left Suez and Bab el-Mandeb reappear almost one-for-one on the Cape of Good Hope route.

The lines cross in 2023-2024: Suez and Bab el-Mandeb both peaked in 2023, then collapsed to roughly half their prior volume as Houthi attacks escalated — while the Cape of Good Hope, which had actually been drifting lower for three years before that (7.9 in 2020 down to 6.2 in 2023), reversed sharply to 9.3 million barrels/day in 2024 and has held near that level since. That inversion, not a single snapshot, is the cleanest evidence the diversion is structural rather than cyclical. Suez container traffic remained 60% below 2023 levels as of the first week of 2026, even after a temporary Houthi pause in late 2025.

SECTION 4

Regional positioning — why Malaysia and not peers

Capturing rerouted mega-vessel traffic requires three specific conditions: deep-water berths, uncommitted capacity, and an established transshipment network. Few Southeast Asian ports satisfy all three simultaneously.

MarketAssessment
Malaysia — Port Klang / PTPDeep water, established infrastructure, and spare berth capacity precisely when Singapore had none to offer.
SingaporeDefault regional hub, but operating near capacity; the source of the 2024 congestion, not a release valve for it.
IndonesiaShares the opposite shore of the Strait of Malacca but lacks a comparable deep-water transshipment terminal.
Vietnam / ThailandServe different trade-lane geometry — strong on intra-Asia flows, positioned off the direct Asia-Europe artery.

With Hormuz under partial blockade and the Red Sea under renewed threat, the Strait of Malacca stands as one of the few major chokepoints not currently under direct military pressure — and Malaysia's terminals sit directly on it.

Capacity investment

Port Klang Authority is implementing a phased tariff increase — 15% from July 2025, a further 10% from January 2026, and 5% in January 2027 — partly funding the Westports 2 expansion, expected to roughly double handling capacity toward 28 million TEU. The scale of investment implies management is underwriting this volume shift as durable rather than transitory.

SECTION 6

Company profile — Westports Holdings Berhad

Westports operates Port Klang's largest container terminal, listed on Bursa Malaysia's Main Market. Revenue is earned per container movement regardless of destination, making transshipment volume — cargo that never enters the Malaysian economy — the segment most directly levered to global rerouting.

The tell

Sell-side coverage explicitly models a rapid return to normal Suez Canal transits as a congestion risk to Westports' own volume guidance. If normalization is treated as a headwind by the analysts covering the stock, the disruption itself has become embedded in the earnings base.

Management attributed the 1Q26 throughput softness to a shorter working month and the preliminary impact of the conflict, but told analysts it has observed no significant Iran-war impact beyond that. The earnings beat itself was driven almost entirely by the scheduled 10% tariff hike effective January 2026, layered on the 15% increase from July 2025 — not by incremental war-driven volume. Fuel is a live cost pressure: it made up 19% of operating expenditure in 1Q26, and Westports has ordered 60 electric trucks, roughly 10% of its fleet, to begin cutting diesel consumption from the third quarter of 2026.

The other side of the trade

RHB Research puts the stock at 15.9 times price-to-earnings and argues conflict-related risk is largely priced in already, with further upside contingent on Malaysia's Transport Ministry approving a diesel cost pass-through mechanism — a live but unresolved policy question, not a certainty.

SECTION 7

Risks to thesis

01

Suez normalization. Maersk and CMA CGM have both made tentative moves back to Suez routings when security conditions permitted. A durable ceasefire reverses the entire mechanism described above.

02

No confirmed war-driven volume yet. Westports' Q1 2026 throughput fell 1.1% y/y (transshipment +0.7%, gateway -3.4%), and management reported no material impact from the Iran war to date. The quarter's earnings beat — 28.4% above HLIB's full-year estimate — was driven by scheduled tariff increases, not incremental demand. Diesel costs, up 17% y/y, are a live headwind the port is only beginning to offset with its electric truck fleet.

03

Tanjung Pelepas growth is alliance-driven. Management attributes the majority of its 13.8% throughput growth to becoming the Gemini Cooperation's Asia hub — a network decision independent of, and pre-dating, the current conflict.

04

Ownership concentration. Roughly two-thirds of the register sits with a single family estate and one strategic institutional holder, a different governance and liquidity profile than a widely-held index constituent.
