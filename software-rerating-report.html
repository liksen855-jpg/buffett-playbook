01 SETTING THE SCENE — MACRO

Why the Market Was Ripe for a Software Comeback

Before we talk about individual stocks, we need to understand the environment we are operating in. Markets don't move in isolation. Software didn't recover because a few companies had good earnings. It recovered because the macro backdrop quietly shifted — and most people missed it.

Three things converged. First, the rate environment stabilised. Growth stocks need falling or stable rates to breathe — when rates are rising, investors discount future earnings more aggressively and high-multiple stocks get punished. The stabilisation gave software room to re-expand its multiples.

Second, the US-Iran geopolitical shock sent investors fleeing into real assets — energy, gold, defensives. This caused a broad market selloff that dragged software down even further than its fundamentals justified. When that fear faded, money had to find a home. And software was sitting at generational lows.

Third, and most importantly — risk appetite returned. When investors feel safe, they reach for growth. Software is the purest growth trade in the market. These three forces didn't just create a bounce. They created the conditions for a full-scale sector rerating.

"Stabilising rates. Fading geopolitical fear. Risk appetite returning. You couldn't have designed a better entry point for beaten-down growth stocks if you tried."

02 THE SECTOR ROTATION STORY

How Software Became the Most Hated Sector in the Market

Here is something most investors forget: the best opportunities are always hiding inside the most hated narratives. And in the first half of 2026, there was no narrative more hated than enterprise software.

The SaaSpocalypse. The fear was simple and terrifying — AI agents would replace every SaaS subscription. Why pay for software when AI does the job for free? Salesforce fell 26% despite a 24.9% earnings beat. ServiceNow fell 9–11% in single sessions despite intact subscription growth. The fear was vivid. The selloff was brutal.

The institutional rotation. Money physically had to go somewhere. It went into semiconductors, AI infrastructure, and mega-cap tech. Nvidia. Broadcom. The picks-and-shovels of AI. Software got left behind entirely as institutions chased the AI infrastructure trade.

The technicals amplified it. The IGV — the iShares Expanded Tech-Software ETF — fell over 30% from its September 2025 highs. Its RSI dipped below 20. To put that in context: that is the most oversold reading in nearly 30 years. The selling had become self-reinforcing, completely detached from the underlying fundamentals of the businesses.

That is your setup. Extreme pessimism. Extreme undervaluation. Institutions underweight. And fundamentals that hadn't actually deteriorated. The SaaSpocalypse was a fear, not a fact.

03 THE CATALYST — WHAT CHANGED

Earnings Season Flipped the Narrative Overnight

The turning point wasn't a Fed announcement. It wasn't a macro data point. It was earnings calls. Specifically — the language on those earnings calls.

Company after company sat down with analysts and said the same thing: AI isn't killing our business. AI is our business.

SINGLE-NAME CATALYST MOVES · Q1 2026 EARNINGS

Snowflake was the landmark moment. The company announced a $6 billion cloud and AI deal with Amazon, surged 36% — its best single day on record. The CEO told analysts: "We're seeing customers deploy and scale AI workloads at a faster pace."

That single data point reframed everything. The market had priced in disruption. It forgot to price in adoption. Enterprise customers aren't replacing SaaS — they're buying more software to manage, deploy, and extract value from AI.

The options market noticed immediately. Call volumes on IGV ran four-to-one against puts. More than 50,000 calls traded in a single session versus under 6,000 puts. Smart money was repositioning in real time.

04 THE FRAMEWORK — TWO LEGS

Understanding Where We Actually Are in This Trade

The most important thing to understand right now is that this rerating has two distinct legs. They require completely different thinking. Most people confuse them — and that confusion is expensive.

LEG ONE
The Relief Rally
COMPLETE

Software survived. The SaaSpocalypse didn't materialise. Valuations that had been compressed on existential fear snapped back as that fear proved overblown. IGV +29% from April lows. This leg rewarded the early believers — the ones who bought into maximum pessimism.

LEG TWO
The Growth Rerating
IN PROGRESS

The market stops asking "will these companies survive AI?" and starts asking "how much will they grow because of AI?" This is where new revenue lines get priced in. Where AI monetisation becomes a real earnings driver. This leg rewards the patient — and the ones paying attention right now.

Here is the key insight: the IGV is still down roughly 19% for the year. The Nasdaq is up 18%. That gap — nearly 37 percentage points — doesn't exist because software is structurally broken. It exists because institutions are still underweight after rotating out in 2025. When they come back — and they have to come back — they don't buy a little. They buy a lot.

05 THE PULLBACK THESIS

Why a Dip Is the Entry, Not the Exit

Before we go further — a note of honesty. A pullback is coming. Almost certainly. The IGV is up nearly 30% from its April lows. ServiceNow ran 14% in a single day. These moves don't go in a straight line, and anyone telling you otherwise is selling something.

The leveraged ETFs in particular — NOWL, CRWL, ZETX — need sustained upward momentum to work properly. A sideways or choppy market eats them alive through daily decay. If you missed the first leg, chasing here with full size is not the move.

08 THE RISK FRAMEWORK

What Kills This Thesis — and When to Worry

Every conviction trade has a kill condition. Here are the four scenarios that would force a re-evaluation of the software thesis.

EARNINGS DISAPPOINTMENT
If Q2 2026 earnings fail to show AI revenue acceleration, the growth rerating thesis collapses. The relief rally was priced on survival. Leg two requires proof of growth.
LEVERAGED ETF DECAY
NOWL, CRWL, ZETX need upward momentum to work. A prolonged sideways or choppy market erodes these positions through daily rebalancing costs. Not a thesis killer — but a position sizing issue.
VALUATION STRETCH
After a 30%+ move, some names are approaching fair value on current estimates. If the growth acceleration doesn't materialise in the numbers, multiples compress again.
MACRO DETERIORATION
A rate spike, a recession signal, or a fresh geopolitical shock would send money back into defensives. Growth stocks get hit first and hardest in risk-off environments.
09 THE VERDICT

Is It Too Late? The Decision Tree.

The honest answer is: it depends entirely on which part of the trade you're entering. Here is how to think through it.

If you believe leg two exists and AI monetisation is real…
No, it's not too late. You're buying momentum, not value — but the fundamental thesis is actually stronger now than it was in April, because earnings have begun to confirm it.
If you want leveraged single-stock ETF exposure…
Wait for a pullback. The risk/reward on chasing here with full size is worse than April. The thesis is intact — but entry point matters with leveraged instruments.
If you want lower-risk software exposure…
IGV still has room. The ETF is still down roughly 19% YTD while the Nasdaq is up 18%. A diversified software exposure through IGV captures the sector rerating without single-stock or leverage risk.
If you're not sure and want to wait…
That's actually fine. A pullback is coming. The people who wait for the reset and buy into weakness are the ones who catch leg two at the right price. Patience here is a strategy, not indecision.
