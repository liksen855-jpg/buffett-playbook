Gold: the price broke, the buyers underneath it didn't

Gold has fallen 22% from January's record while the structural bid — central banks, reserve managers, long-duration holders — barely moved. This report maps why the selloff happened, where accumulation is occurring, and the two dates in July that decide what comes next.

Key findings

The five conclusions that matter, before the detail
1
The selloff was a rates event, not a gold event
The 22% drawdown from January's $5,355 record was driven by leveraged funds repricing Federal Reserve policy — first the March positioning washout, then June's hike scare after inflation printed 4.2%. The structural demand base did not participate in the selling.
2
The structural bid held through the entire drawdown
Global gold ETF holdings fell roughly 1% from their February record despite a one-fifth price decline. Central banks returned to net buying in May (+41 tonnes), and China's official buying streak extended past 19 consecutive months. Holders stepped aside; they did not exit.
3
Speculative positioning is washed out
Money-manager net longs were cut 18% by late February and total COMEX net length sat in neutral territory by end-May. The crowded-trade risk that made January dangerous is not present at $4,100. That converts dips into accumulation setups rather than distribution.
4
$3,900–4,100 has been defended twice
June 24 ($4,009 settlement) and the turn of the month both saw buyers absorb supply at this shelf — the same zone where the November 2025 base was built. Below it, $3,886 is where the correction thesis breaks.
5
July 14 and July 29 are the fulcrum
Last week's payrolls miss (57k vs ~114k expected) cut September hike odds from two-thirds to a coin flip and produced the bounce off $4,000. A soft CPI on July 14 confirms the floor; a hot one re-arms the hike trade and reopens the $3,700s.
I.

Anatomy of the decline

What changed in the last year, quarter, month, and week — kept separate on purpose

Gold's 2026 has been a story of one market wearing two prices: the price set by traders reacting to the Federal Reserve, and the price paid by institutions accumulating reserves. For six months, the first price fell while the second kept buying.

The year

Gold gained roughly 65% in 2025, then went parabolic into late January 2026 — settling at a record $5,354.80 on January 29 on the year's heaviest volume (intraday prints ran higher, to roughly $5,586–5,595 depending on venue). The next session it crashed 11% in a single day, a textbook blowoff top. The market then rebuilt, briefly reclaiming the settlement high at $5,366.50 on March 1 before the March breakdown began, and ETF holdings hit an all-time record of 4,176 tonnes on February 27.

The quarter

Two forces dominated Q2. First, a ~15% March break ($5,062 → $4,376 in under two weeks) that Goldman characterized as a leveraged-positioning unwind rather than a fundamental rupture. Second, the US–Iran conflict and Strait of Hormuz disruption — the shipping lane carrying roughly a fifth of the world's oil — pushed Brent crude toward $120, which kept inflation sticky, which kept the Fed hawkish, which kept real yields high. Q2 became gold's worst quarterly decline since the second quarter of 2013.

The month

June was the capitulation leg. Strong June 5 payrolls plus a June 10 CPI print of 4.2% flipped the market from pricing cuts to pricing a possible hike. The 10-year TIPS real yield — the return on a government bond after inflation, gold's direct competitor — reached 2.28%, and the dollar hit thirteen-month highs. Gold broke $4,000 for the first time since November 2025. Goldman cut its year-end target from $5,400 to $4,900 on June 20.

The week

The hawkish premise cracked. July 1: soft ADP payrolls and a less hawkish tone from Fed Chair Warsh at the ECB forum. July 2: the economy added just 57,000 jobs — the fewest in four months, versus ~114,000 expected — and September hike odds fell from two-thirds to roughly even. Gold jumped over 2% in a day, while Hormuz shipping resumed and oil returned to pre-war levels near $69 WTI.

II.

The levels that matter

Where buyers act, where the thesis breaks, and where the trend repairs

Production cost is not the floor here. Industry median all-in sustaining cost (what it costs miners to produce an ounce, all expenses included) sits near $1,500–1,650. Gold would need to fall over 55% before the cost curve matters. The relevant thresholds are behavioral and monetary, not geological.

III.

Institutional positioning

Who sold, who held, who's still buying — and one data conflict worth naming

A 22% drawdown with intact institutional holdings is the signature of a positioning washout, not a broken thesis.

Central banks. From 2021–2025, official institutions bought an average of 225 tonnes per quarter — roughly double the prior five-year pace. The World Gold Council reported central banks back in net buying mode in May, adding 41 tonnes, and China's central bank extended its reported buying streak past 19 consecutive months. Chinese net gold imports hit 317 tonnes in Q1 — nearly triple the prior quarter.

Flagged — the reported-vs-estimated gap

On a reported basis, net Q1 central bank purchases were only 16 tonnes, with 129 tonnes of gross selling headlined by Türkiye's 60-tonne March sale. Using London OTC and Swiss refinery flow data, the World Gold Council estimates total Q1 buying at 244 tonnes — up from Q4. Both figures are presented because the unreported component cannot be independently verified.

ETFs. February saw $5.3bn of inflows — the ninth consecutive month of North American inflows. May slowed to modest net outflows and June brought the first Asian monthly outflow since August 2025. But the stock, not the flow, is the story: holdings of roughly 4,121 tonnes sit barely 1% below the record, through the entire drawdown.

Futures positioning. Money-manager net longs fell 18% to 311 tonnes by late February; by end-May, total COMEX net length of 466 tonnes sat in neutral territory — and managed money actually added length in three of May's four weeks.

Forward intent. An OMFIF survey of 90 central banks and sovereign funds published June 30 found, for the first time on record, more institutions planning to cut dollar allocations than raise them over the coming decade, with a net 30% planning to increase gold within two years.

IV.

The asymmetric layer

Tail scenarios, the structural thesis, and the honest bear case

The stagflation setup markets are not pricing cleanly. Last week produced 57,000 jobs alongside 4.2% inflation. The consensus debate is binary: hawkish Fed versus cuts. The third scenario is a Fed unable to move in either direction — a rate hold driven by inflation paralysis rather than confidence. That loss of monetary agency is historically gold's best regime.

Tail scenarios favoring gold. Renewed Hormuz escalation, a hard-landing signal forcing emergency easing into 4% inflation, a fiscal or Treasury-market stress event, or accelerated reserve diversification. Each is low-probability individually; gold is the asset that gets paid on all of them simultaneously.

The structural thesis, separate from price. The 2022 freezing of Russian reserves demonstrated that offshore currency assets carry counterparty risk — they can be frozen by someone else's decision — while gold in a domestic vault cannot. Supply is inelastic: new mines take seven to ten years from discovery to production.

The honest bear case. ING and BMO both cut H2 targets in June; OCBC sees declines through end-2026 on firm yields and a strong dollar. That scenario requires growth and inflation re-accelerating together — a high bar the July 2 payrolls miss raised further. But a hot July 14 CPI would revive it within hours.

VI.

Synthesis — the accumulation framework

How the layers combine into a plan rather than a prediction

The selloff was made by the layer of the market that always overshoots. The layer that doesn't — reserve managers, long-duration holders, physical buyers — held its ground. That divergence, plus a twice-defended floor and washed-out speculative positioning, defines the setup.

Where to accumulate
In tranches on tests of $3,900–4,100 — the zone institutions have defended twice — rather than chasing strength into resistance ahead of two binary events.
How to size
As portfolio insurance, not a directional bet: the industry-standard 5–15% allocation range, scaled to tolerance for a possible $3,700s tail if July 14 runs hot.
What confirms
A weekly close above the 200-day moving average (~$4,600). Until then, treat strength as range-trading, not trend.
What invalidates
A weekly close below $3,886. At that point the correction thesis is wrong.
What to watch
July 14 CPI, July 29 FOMC, Iran transit-fee negotiations, and the WGC's Q2 central-bank data.

The realistic scenario is not a straight line in either direction. It is a floor being built by slow money while fast money finishes repricing the Fed — with the decisive information arriving on two known dates.
