Business & Thesis

  • What it does: designs the GPUs and AI accelerators that train and run the world's AI models — commands an estimated 80–90% share of the AI accelerator market by revenue.
  • The re-rating: forward multiple compressed from 51x to roughly 20–22x over the past year, even as revenue grew 85% and the company posted its 15th consecutive quarterly revenue beat and 14th straight EPS beat (Q1 FY27, reported May 2026).
  • Visibility: management has flagged roughly $500B of revenue visibility across the Blackwell and Rubin generations — booked demand, not a forecast.
  • Positioning: trading below the Street's consensus average target (~$284–301 across 22–68 analysts), alongside a fresh $80B buyback authorization and optionality from potential China re-entry.
  • Moat: the CUDA software ecosystem — switching away from Nvidia means rewriting the entire AI stack, not just swapping a chip.

Risk Factors

  • Custom silicon: hyperscaler in-house chips (Google TPU, AWS Trainium, Broadcom ASICs) are the fastest-growing competitive threat, projected toward 10–15% share by 2026.
  • AMD encroachment: still small (5–8% share) but growing every quarter.
  • Capacity/export risk: China policy and TSMC allocation remain outside management's control.

Business & Thesis

  • What it does: sells advertising across Facebook, Instagram, and WhatsApp to roughly 3.5–3.6 billion daily active people.
  • Advantage+: Meta's AI-driven ad automation tool has scaled to an estimated $60B annual run-rate on its own.
  • The drawdown: stock is down ~28% from its 52-week high of $796, and down 14.7% YTD while the S&P 500 is up ~9.6% — a rare underperformance stretch for the name.
  • Moat: daily-active-user scale (3.5B+) generates a behavioral data advantage that's genuinely difficult for rivals to replicate in ad targeting.

Risk Factors

  • Capex payoff: the market's central fear — $125–145B of 2026 capital spending needs to convert into revenue, not just infrastructure.
  • Reality Labs: continues to post large operating losses with no clear monetization timeline.
  • Regulatory: ongoing antitrust and privacy exposure across the US and EU.

Uber Technologies, Inc.
Core — priced for disaster, growing like dominance

Trips up 22%, bookings up 21%, two straight quarters of accelerating active riders — while the market keeps pricing in a robotaxi extinction event that hasn't shown up in the numbers.

Business & Thesis

  • What it does: the largest combined ride-hail and delivery platform in the West — a two-sided marketplace where more riders attract more drivers, which shortens wait times and attracts more riders.
  • Growth: trips +22%, gross bookings +21%, monthly active platform consumers accelerating for two consecutive quarters.
  • The reframe: we view Uber as the likely autonomy aggregator — the demand layer robotaxi fleets will plug into — rather than a company being disrupted by autonomy.
  • Cash generation: roughly a 6.5% free-cash-flow yield on a business still compounding bookings at 20–25% a year.
  • Sponsorship: Uber is Bill Ackman's single largest disclosed equity position (~15.7% of Pershing Square's 13F as of Q1 2026) — trimmed modestly last quarter, but still the anchor holding.

Risk Factors

  • Autonomy disintermediation: the bear case is that Waymo, Tesla, or others bypass Uber's marketplace entirely and go direct to consumer.
  • Regulatory: driver classification fights remain live across multiple jurisdictions.
  • Margin risk: delivery segment economics remain thinner than mobility.

Business & Thesis

  • What it does: sells enterprise software (Office, Windows), the Azure cloud platform, and — increasingly — Copilot AI seats.
  • Booked demand: commercial remaining performance obligation hit $627B, up 99% YoY — revenue that's already contracted, not projected.
  • Azure: grew 40% in the most recent quarter (39% constant currency), guided to 39–40% next quarter — capacity-constrained, not demand-constrained.
  • Copilot: surpassed 20 million paid seats, with seat adds up 250% year-over-year — the fastest growth since launch. AI business overall crossed a $37B annual run-rate, up 123%.
  • Valuation: cheapest forward earnings multiple in roughly three years, despite the accelerating AI numbers.
  • Optionality: Microsoft's stake in OpenAI (~27%) means it's paid whether OpenAI wins, loses, or gets disrupted by a competitor.

Risk Factors

  • Capex load: guided to ~$190B in calendar 2026 capital expenditure, with ~$25B of that from component price inflation alone.
  • Margin compression: Microsoft Cloud gross margin has slipped from 70.1% to roughly 68% as AI investment ramps.
  • OpenAI concentration: a large piece of the AI narrative rests on one partner's trajectory.
  • Regulatory: an active FTC probe remains a background risk.

Business & Thesis

  • What it does: a multi-domain autonomous-defense roll-up assembled over roughly a year — attack and surveillance drones, counter-drone cyber defense, stratospheric balloons, robotic minefield clearance, AI battlefield software (Omnisys), and private wireless networks.
  • Growth: revenue jumped from $4.25M to $50.12M year-over-year (+1,080%), almost entirely from acquisitions rather than organic expansion.
  • Balance sheet: roughly $1.48B in cash and a $457M backlog, alongside a Mistral prime-contractor relationship giving access to U.S. defense procurement.
  • Macro tailwind: positioned into the Pentagon's roughly $75B drone-modernization cycle.
  • Price action: down more than 45% from its 52-week high of $15.28, currently around $8.24 — well below the $11.00–11.28 technical zone we'd previously flagged.

Risk Factors

  • Not yet profitable: the headline $361M GAAP net income figure is mostly non-cash accounting gains; adjusted EBITDA was actually a $10.9M loss.
  • Complexity: six-plus business lines assembled in under a year — no multi-year track record to underwrite the "moat" thesis yet.
  • Lumpy revenue: inorganic, acquisition-driven growth is much harder to model forward than organic growth.
  • Broken technical structure: price has fallen through both the entry zone and the stop-loss level from the original swing-trade setup — the chart no longer supports the trade it was built on.

This is the most important table on the page to read carefully. Only 3 of 10 methods could even be calculated — the rest require profitability metrics Ondas doesn't have yet. And the three that did run disagree by nearly 40x with each other: net-income-based DNI-20 says $72.87, revenue-growth-based methods say $1.96–$4.20. The $26.34 composite is a trimmed mean of three wildly disagreeing numbers, not a converged estimate. Treat it as "somewhere between deeply undervalued and overvalued, we genuinely don't know yet" rather than a precise target.

Bottom Line

The fundamental growth story is real. The valuation model can't yet confirm or deny whether the stock is cheap, because the business hasn't existed in its current form long enough to value conventionally. Size accordingly — this is a speculative slot, not a core holding.
