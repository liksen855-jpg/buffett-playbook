02

What Actually Happened

The selloff decoded

Over $1 trillion was wiped from semiconductor stocks in two days. Here is exactly what triggered it, why it spread, and — critically — what it doesn't mean for the six stocks in this brief.

On June 4, Broadcom reported Q2 FY2026 results. AI chip revenue grew 200% year over year. But their Q3 guidance came in at $16 billion — against analyst whisper numbers of $17.2 billion. That $1.2 billion shortfall against an expectation, not against the official consensus, was enough to trigger a sector-wide selloff. The following day, a hotter-than-expected US jobs report added the macro pressure: strong employment data means the Fed holds rates higher, which compresses high-growth multiples. The combination sent the SOX down 8.5% in a single session.

What this was

A sentiment reset after nine weeks of near-uninterrupted gains. Profits were taken. Whisper numbers weren't met. The jobs report gave institutional sellers the macro cover they needed to reduce exposure. This is standard market mechanics after an extended rally — not a signal about the underlying AI infrastructure buildout.

What this was not

A fundamental breakdown. Not one hyperscaler cancelled a data center. Not one AI roadmap was revised. Jensen Huang called Marvell "the next trillion-dollar company" six days before this selloff and has not walked it back. NVIDIA's $2 billion investment in Marvell has not changed. The Nebius–Bloom Energy $2.6 billion power deal signed May 14 has not changed.

Sentiment resets create entries. Fundamental breakdowns require exits. Every name in this brief experienced the former. None experienced the latter. That distinction is the entire basis for this report.

Where each name sits in the AI stack

These six stocks occupy four distinct, non-duplicating layers of the AI infrastructure chain. Each layer has a different risk profile, a different growth driver, and a different catalyst calendar. Understanding the map prevents you from treating them as interchangeable.

Navitas doesn't compete with Bloom — one powers the chip, the other powers the building. A rising AI capex tide lifts all four layers simultaneously, which is why a sector-wide selloff presents an opportunity across the whole map.

Competitive Moat

Why the hyperscalers can't just replace them

A stock at a good price is not enough. The question is whether the revenue is defensible — whether switching costs, network effects, or structural positioning make each company difficult to displace. Here is the honest answer for each name.

$MRVL Wide Moat
Design-in

Custom silicon is a multi-year lock-in. Once a hyperscaler designs Marvell's XPU into their AI cluster architecture, the switching cost is enormous — every layer of software, toolchain, and interconnect is built around that chip. Ripping it out means rebuilding the entire stack.

Scale

20+ custom chip design wins already in pipeline for FY2028–29. The revenue flywheel is being loaded now. Each design win adds another multi-year program — the business compounds through volume, not one-off contracts.

NVIDIA

$2B NVIDIA investment and NVLink Fusion integration creates a structural alliance, not just a partnership. NVIDIA has a financial incentive to route hyperscaler demand through Marvell's custom silicon platform.

Moat strength
$CRDO Wide Moat
Switch

Active electrical cables are a qualification-intensive product. Hyperscalers run extensive interoperability and reliability testing before deploying AECs at scale. Once qualified across a data center fleet, Credo's cables are not easily swapped — the certification process alone takes 12–18 months.

TAM

Demand scales directly with GPU density. Every GPU added to every cluster adds connectivity demand — Credo's TAM is structurally tied to the AI capex buildout, not a fixed install base. The more the hyperscalers spend, the bigger the addressable market.

Risk

~39% revenue from one customer is a real concentration risk. This is not hidden — it is the primary bear case. Diversification is the key thing to watch in future earnings calls.

Moat strength
$NVTS Emerging
IP

GaNFast platform is proprietary IP. Navitas's monolithic GaN integration — combining power, drive, control, and protection on one chip — is patented and not easily replicated. Gallium nitride process expertise takes years to develop at yield.

NVIDIA

MGX ecosystem integration is a structural position. Being inside NVIDIA's reference architecture means every OEM building to that spec is a potential customer. The moat is being built now — it does not exist at scale yet, which is both the risk and the opportunity.

Risk

Still loss-making at $8.6M quarterly revenue. The moat thesis is forward-looking. Texas Instruments, Infineon, and ON Semi are larger, better-capitalised competitors in power semiconductors. Execution matters enormously here.

Moat strength
$QCOM Mixed
Licensing

CDMA and 5G patent portfolio generates royalty revenue regardless of chip wins. Every smartphone sold globally — including competitors' devices — pays Qualcomm. This annuity-like licensing stream is genuinely difficult to displace and provides a revenue floor independent of design competition.

Auto

Snapdragon Ride is deeply embedded in automotive roadmaps. With $1.33B in quarterly automotive revenue and growing, Qualcomm has multi-year design commitments from OEMs. Automotive design cycles run 4–7 years — once in, they are difficult to displace.

Risk

Data center moat is unproven. The custom silicon thesis is forward-looking. NVIDIA entering the PC chip market is a genuine new competitive threat in Qualcomm's core smartphone segment. The moat is strong in licensing and auto; speculative in data center.

Moat strength
$MXL Building
Certif.

Optical IC qualification cycles are long and sticky. Data center operators test MaxLinear's SerDes and optical ICs extensively before deployment. The PCIe 7.0 and 800G ethernet ramps require re-qualification — and MaxLinear is already in that process with hyperscalers, giving them a first-mover advantage in the next-generation connectivity cycle.

Ramp

Revenue inflection only just started. The moat is being built as the business scales — $137M in Q1 is the start, not the peak. The question is whether MaxLinear can convert the current hyperscaler relationships into multi-year program wins before larger competitors move in.

Risk

Still GAAP loss-making. Marvell, Broadcom, and Cisco are all competing in data center optical — MaxLinear's moat is real but narrow. Execution over the next two quarters is critical to establishing durable customer relationships.

Moat strength
$BE Structural
Contracts

10-year supply contracts are structural lock-in. The Nebius deal runs ten years per phase. The Oracle 2.8 GW deal is multi-year. Once a data center is built around Bloom's fuel cells — with site infrastructure, maintenance agreements, and power delivery architecture designed around them — the switching cost is the data center itself.

Agnostic

Power demand is model-agnostic and chip-agnostic. It does not matter whether NVIDIA, AMD, or custom ASICs win the AI compute race — the data center still needs electricity. Bloom's moat is not tied to any single technology outcome, which makes it uniquely resilient among the six names.

Backlog

FY2026 revenue guidance raised to $3.4–3.8B. The order backlog is growing faster than the business can deploy. Grid queue constraints that frustrate competitors actually strengthen Bloom's position — their on-site model is the only solution that doesn't require waiting a decade for grid access.

Bloom Energy is not a semiconductor company. It is in this brief because it addresses the binding constraint that limits everything else on this list: power. AI data centers need massive, always-on electricity. US data center power demand is projected to reach 9–17% of the US grid by 2030 — up from 4–5% today (EPRI 2026). Grid interconnection queues run 5–10 years.

Bloom's solid oxide fuel cells generate electricity on-site, bypassing the grid entirely. On May 14, 2026 (per SEC filing), Nebius Group signed a Master Fuel Cell Capacity Agreement with Bloom — $2.6B in service fees over the term, 250MW guaranteed capacity, 328MW installed, three phases each with a 10-year supply term. Bloom's stock rose more than 11% on the announcement. Separately, Bloom has a 2.8 GW expanded deal with Oracle for AI cloud infrastructure. FY2026 revenue guidance was raised to $3.4–3.8B.

The most important data point from last week: when the SOX fell 8.5%, Bloom barely moved. Power demand is model-agnostic — it doesn't matter which chip wins, which AI model dominates, or what the Fed does with rates. The data centers still need electricity.
