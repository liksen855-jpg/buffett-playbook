Simpan SSPN Prime
Issued by PTPTN · National Education Savings Scheme

What it actually is

You hand PTPTN the money and, under an Islamic contract called Wakalah Bil Istithmar (agency-for-investment), they invest it on your behalf — closer to a government-managed unit trust than a deposit account. The "i" stands for Islamic; Shariah-structured by design, open to every Malaysian regardless of religion.

Who can open it

  • Child: Malaysian citizen, 1 day old up to 18.
  • Depositor: biological parent, adoptive parent, or legal guardian only.
  • No income test on the depositor, no cap on accounts across multiple children.

AMANAH SAHAM MALAYSIA

ACCT TYPE: ASM · UNIT PRICE RM1.00

What it actually is

A government-linked unit trust from ASNB/PNB, priced flat at RM1.00 a unit. Balance doesn't swing — instead you receive one annual income distribution, declared in sen per unit.

Who can open it

  • Open to every Malaysian citizen, any ethnicity.
  • Guardian applies on behalf of a minor with a valid birth certificate — confirmed in ASNB's own product sheet. Works from birth.
  • RM10 minimum initial investment.

ASB vs ASM

ASB isn't "Malay-only" — restricted to Bumiputera status, a broader category including Sabah/Sarawak native groups. ASM is the open-to-all equivalent.

Why it needs a trustee at all

A minor can't legally enter a binding contract in Malaysia, so a one-year-old can't hold a brokerage account directly. A parent opens it instead, as trustee — the assets are the child's, the parent operates the account until they're grown. This is the one account here that isn't an off-the-shelf product with published rules, which is exactly why most parents never get round to it.

Two real advantages

  • No capital gains tax on shares, for individuals. Malaysia's 2024 capital gains tax applies to companies, LLPs, and trust bodies disposing of unlisted shares. Individuals sit outside its scope, and shares listed on Bursa or a foreign exchange are exempt regardless. Growth compounds untouched for 18 years.
  • No "kiddie tax" rule. Some countries, like Canada or Australia, tax a child's investment income back at the parent's rate on purpose. Malaysia has no equivalent rule — worth treating as general information rather than a guarantee for every account structure, since it can depend on exactly how the account is set up.

The advantage that isn't about tax

The real reason an 18-year account works is that nobody's checking the price. Adult investors tend to sell during downturns — that's usually the single biggest reason real-world returns fall short of what the market actually delivered. A child's account, left alone, doesn't have that problem.

What to put in it

Look for durable, well-run businesses rather than speculative names — steady demand, strong market position, more than a decade of proven results. A few well-known examples that fit this profile: Visa and Mastercard (payments), Costco (membership retail) — each has compounded at roughly 20% a year over the past 15 years.

ImportantThe figures used in the calculator above don't assume 20% — they use a more conservative 10%, because strong past performance from a handful of companies isn't something to plan a child's account around.

Things to watch for

Concentration riskA handful of individual stocks carries more risk than a broad index fund. If simplicity matters more than picking winners, a low-cost global index fund is the safer, more defensible choice.
US-listed sharesNeeds a broker with US market access, not a standard local-only account — factor in currency conversion and US dividend withholding tax.
Regulation riskPayment companies like Visa and Mastercard face ongoing regulatory scrutiny over the fees they charge merchants — a real, recurring risk to their business model.
Confirm before fundingCheck the trust or nominee structure directly with the broker first — being legally possible and being something the broker actually processes smoothly aren't always the same thing.

Two things worth knowing

One bonus, one myth

Tabung Haji — if your family is Muslim

A fourth account most people never file under "wealth"

Filed mentally under "the Hajj fund" so it rarely appears in finance lists — but opens from birth with a birth certificate and MyKid, 100% government-guaranteed, tax-exempt hibah. Recent distribution 3.5%. Past RM1,300 saved, the child is auto-registered in the Hajj queue — one that can run 10–15+ years joining as an adult.

Depositors must be Muslim — not available to every family.

Myth — "EPF for kids"

KWSP registration opens at age 14, requires the child's own MyKad. No infant pathway; top-ups still need an already-registered member. If EPF belongs in the plan, it's opened at 14, not year one.

If you do nothing else

The order to do this in

  • Week one: open SSPN-i online. RM20, ~10 minutes, no documents. Guaranteed and tax-deductible.
  • Week two: open ASM (or ASB if Bumiputera) with RM10. Set a standing instruction.
  • Month one to three: the broker account. Confirm trust structure, pick a broker with US access, set a deadline.
  • Every year after: claim the SSPN relief — it isn't automatic.

Sources & method

How this was fact-checked

Every figure below was checked against PTPTN, ASNB, or LHDN's own materials where possible, cross-checked against at least one independent source, and re-verified specifically for this report rather than carried over from general knowledge.

SSPN-i

  • RM8,000 relief, per household, through YA2027: confirmed on PTPTN's Simpan SSPN Plus page, which cites Budget 2025's three-year extension (YA2025–2027).
  • Means-tested matching grant, up to RM10,000, income ≤RM4,000/month: confirmed on DollarsAndSense.my's SSPN breakdown, cross-checked against PTPTN's own page.
  • Free takaful above RM1,000 saved, covers the depositor not the child: confirmed directly on PTPTN's Simpan SSPN Prime page ("Ringgit to Ringgit coverage for general accounts") and independently in The Malaysian Reserve, which reports a real payout case.
  • Nomination bypasses Grant of Probate: confirmed on PTPTN's own page and independently in Bernama's BFokus explainer. One nuance not in earlier drafts of this report: for Muslim depositors the nominee receives the funds "for administration" (still subject to Islamic distribution rules), while non-Muslim nominees receive the funds outright.
  • Required before a PTPTN study loan, since Jan 2012: confirmed independently across five sources, including RinggitPlus's 2026 PTPTN loan guide and PTPTN's own loan info page.
  • 4% dividend assumption: SSPN's actual recent history ranges roughly 3–4.05%; 4% used as a conservative mid-range planning figure, not a promise.

ASM / ASB

  • Guardian may open for a minor with a birth certificate: confirmed in ASNB's own ASM 3 product highlights sheet.
  • Latest distributions (ASB 5.75 sen, ASM 4.75–5.00 sen): from ASNB/PNB's own annual distribution announcements.
  • 10-year distribution history (2016–2025): compiled from a third-party aggregator of PNB's announcements, not PNB's own historical archive directly — the individual years are internally consistent with known market conditions (pre-pandemic peak, 2021–22 trough), but exact figures should be checked at asnb.com.my before being treated as final.
  • ASB restricted to Bumiputera, ASM open to all citizens: confirmed on ASNB's public fund pages.

Broker / trust account

  • No individual capital gains tax on shares: checked against multiple independent Malaysian tax-practice summaries of the 2024 CGT regime; consistent finding that the tax applies to companies, LLPs, and trust bodies disposing of unlisted shares, not individuals, and that listed shares are exempt regardless.
  • Minors cannot hold a binding contract, hence the trust structure: general Malaysian trust-law principle, consistent across sources; not tied to a single named statute in this report — worth confirming the specific mechanism with a broker or advisor before opening.
  • Visa/Mastercard/Costco 15-year CAGR (~20–22%): independently re-verified this round against market-data sources; the range checked out closely, though Visa's 10-year figure in an earlier draft (17.2%) was found to be overstated against independent data (~15%) — the report now avoids citing that specific figure and uses a general "~20%" range instead.
  • No "kiddie tax" attribution rule in Malaysia: general information based on comparison with Canada/Australia's specific attribution rules; Malaysia has no confirmed equivalent, but this is stated as general information, not a guarantee for every account structure — confirm with a tax advisor for significant sums.

Calculator methodology

  • Every projection uses the midpoint of two annual-compounding bounds (deposits credited at year-end vs. year-start), because SSPN and ASNB funds pay dividends once a year, not monthly.
  • At higher assumed rates, a simpler monthly-compounding calculation drifts toward the optimistic end of that range rather than the middle — this report deliberately uses the midpoint instead, which is why the broker account's projection is more conservative than a standard online calculator would show for the same inputs.

What this is not

This is general information compiled and checked in July 2026, not personalised financial, tax, or legal advice. Rates, relief caps, and grant eligibility change — verify current figures directly with PTPTN, ASNB, and LHDN before acting on anything here.
