PTPTN vs EPF: a ten-year ledger

Everyone rushes to kill their PTPTN loan early. We ran the actual numbers — the real dividends, the real Ujrah, the real years — to see if that's actually the smart move.

The Setup

Same money, two decisions

A RM30,000 PTPTN loan, repaid over 15 years, carries a minimum payment of about RM179 a month. Say you have an extra RM300 a month sitting spare. Do you throw it at the loan, or let it grow in EPF while you pay the minimum? We tracked both paths for 10 years, using EPF's real declared dividends.

The Verdict

Investing the surplus wins by RM 7,996

Not the RM30,000 headline you'll see thrown around online — the honest number, once both paths spend the same money and Option A's leftover debt is fairly counted against it. Still a clear, repeatable edge for the same monthly effort.

Why It Happens

PTPTN's Ujrah charge and EPF's average dividend aren't close. The whole edge comes from this gap, sustained every year for a decade.

Read Before You Act
✎ The honest caveats
  • This only works if the surplus is actually invested, consistently, rather than spent. The debt payoff in Option B is guaranteed the moment you make it — the EPF growth in Option A is not.
  • EPF's past dividends are not a promise of future ones. Only Simpanan Konvensional carries a legal floor of 2.5% per year; Simpanan Shariah has no such guarantee.
  • If you're also carrying high-interest debt — credit cards, personal loans — clear that first. PTPTN's 1% Ujrah is nowhere near the most expensive debt in the room.
  • This scenario uses a RM30,000 balance and RM300 surplus as a clean illustration. Your own numbers will move the exact gap — the mechanism, not the figure, is the takeaway.

Figures are illustrative, based on a RM30,000 PTPTN balance at 1% Ujrah and EPF's declared Simpanan Konvensional dividend rates, 2016–2025. Not financial advice — EPF returns are not guaranteed beyond the 2.5% statutory floor. Model your own numbers before making a repayment decision.
