How Markets Work

The mechanical reality of how stocks are bought and sold — exchanges, participants, price discovery, order types, and market structure. Understanding the machine before you use it.

55 min Video · Article · Quiz · Diagram
Section 01

What Is a Stock Market?

A stock market is a marketplace where buyers and sellers exchange ownership stakes in publicly listed companies. When you buy a share of a company, you are purchasing a fractional claim on its assets, earnings, and future cash flows. You become, legally and economically, a part-owner.

The market serves two distinct functions that are often confused:

  • Primary market: Companies raise capital by selling new shares to the public through an Initial Public Offering (IPO). The company receives the proceeds. This is the capital formation function.
  • Secondary market: After the IPO, investors trade shares among themselves on exchanges. The company receives no proceeds from these trades. This is where virtually all daily trading occurs.
Key Concept

When you buy Apple stock on the NYSE, you are not giving Apple any money. You are buying shares from another investor who chose to sell. Apple's role ended at the IPO. The secondary market is simply investors reassessing the value of already-issued shares.

Section 03

Price Discovery & the Order Book

Prices are not assigned — they emerge. Price discovery is the process by which markets aggregate the opinions of all participants about what a security is worth, and express that consensus as a single number: the current price.

The mechanism is the order book — a real-time ledger of all outstanding buy (bid) and sell (ask) orders at every price level. The current market price is simply the intersection of the highest willing buyer and lowest willing seller.

Section 04

Order Types Every Investor Must Know

Your choice of order type is not a technicality — it directly affects execution price, risk, and strategy. These are the four order types that matter:

Order TypeExecutionPrice CertaintyBest For
Market OrderImmediate at best available priceNone — price can slipHigh-liquidity stocks, urgency
Limit OrderOnly at your specified price or betterFull — your price or no dealMost long-term investing
Stop OrderBecomes market order when price hits stopNone after triggerCutting losses, protecting gains
Stop-LimitBecomes limit order when price hits stopPrice certainty after triggerPrecision risk management
Practical Insight

For most long-term investors, limit orders are superior to market orders in virtually all situations. A market order for an illiquid stock during volatile conditions can execute significantly worse than the displayed price. Always use limit orders unless speed of execution is genuinely critical.

Section 05

Market Efficiency & What It Means for You

The Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH) proposes that stock prices at all times reflect all available information. In its strong form, this would mean no investor can consistently beat the market, since any advantage would be instantly arbitraged away.

The evidence is mixed. Markets are mostly efficient for widely-followed, liquid securities. But inefficiencies persist at the edges — small caps, emerging markets, periods of extreme sentiment, and in situations where information asymmetry exists.

Three Forms of Market Efficiency

The practical implication: beating the market in large-cap, well-covered US stocks is extraordinarily difficult. Beating the market in less-efficient areas — emerging markets, microcaps, event-driven situations — is possible but requires genuine edge, not just effort.

Section 06

Bull Markets, Bear Markets & Market Cycles

Markets move in cycles. Understanding cycle vocabulary and mechanics prevents the most common and costly mistake in investing: panic selling at lows and exuberant buying at highs.

  • Bull market: Sustained price rise of 20%+ from recent lows. Characterized by rising confidence, expanding earnings expectations, and often leverage.
  • Bear market: Sustained price decline of 20%+ from recent highs. Characterized by falling earnings, risk aversion, and forced selling.
  • Correction: A 10–20% pullback within a larger bull or bear trend. Often sudden, often a buying opportunity.
  • Crash: Rapid decline of 20%+ in days or weeks. Typically driven by liquidity events, not fundamentals.
Historical Context

The S&P 500 has experienced a bear market (–20%+) approximately every 3.5 years on average. It has recovered and made new highs every single time. The investor who understood this and did not sell in 2009, 2020, or 2022 made extraordinary returns. The one who panicked made losses permanent.
