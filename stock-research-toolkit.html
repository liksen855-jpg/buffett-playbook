Prompt 01
Equity Research Report

Turn Claude into a sell-side analyst. It breaks down the entire business and finds catalysts the market hasn't priced in — including ones that aren't obvious.

What's inside
Revenue model & segment breakdown
Cash flow & financial health
Valuation vs. peers
Macro & regulatory environment
Asymmetric & hidden catalysts
Risk assessment
Bull vs. bear investment thesis
1
Business model breakdown
How does the company make money? Break revenue into segments — advertising, subscriptions, licensing, other. Assess whether the model is sustainable.
2
Financial health check
Cash flow dynamics, debt structure, working capital, and balance sheet quality. Is the company burning cash or generating it?
3
Valuation vs. competitors
P/E, EV/Revenue, Price/Book. How does this stock compare to peers? Is it expensive, cheap, or fairly priced relative to growth?
4
Macro & regulatory environment
What external forces — interest rates, ad market cycles, regulation — affect this stock? What's the competitive threat landscape?
5
Catalysts — obvious & hidden
Asymmetric: low-probability, high-impact events like partnerships or unexpected profitability. Hidden: drivers the market is undervaluing right now.
6
Bull vs. bear — both fairly argued
Claude presents the strongest version of each case before concluding. No cheerleading — evidence-based with clear risk flags.

Prompt 02
3-Statement Financial Model

Real numbers from SEC filings. A 5-year projection with every assumption labelled. The same model investment bankers build — in 60 seconds.

What's inside
3 years of real historical data
5-year revenue projections
Income statement build
Balance sheet with balance check
Cash flow tied to balance sheet
All assumptions clearly labelled
Bear / base / bull sensitivity table
1
Pull 3 years of real data
From SEC filings: revenue, expenses, assets, debt, equity, cash flow. Every number cites the exact filing and year — no made-up figures.
2
Project revenue (5 years)
Growth rate justified by historical CAGR or analyst consensus. Three scenarios: bear (−5%), base, bull (+5%). Math shown step by step.
3
Build the income statement
Gross margin, R&D, SG&A, depreciation, taxes — all as % of revenue using 3-year averages. Net income calculated per year.
4
Build the balance sheet
Working capital driven by how fast the company collects cash and pays suppliers. Every year must balance: Assets = Liabilities + Equity.
5
Build the cash flow statement
Net income → add depreciation → subtract capex = free cash flow. Ending cash must tie exactly to the balance sheet.
6
Check everything links + output
All 3 statements verified to connect. Output: 8-column tables (3 historical + 5 projected), assumptions table, Year 5 FCF sensitivity at ±3%.

Prompt 03
Bear Case Stress Test

The prompt most people skip. Forces Claude to argue the most credible downside — so you know your exit before you're even in the trade.

What's inside
What the market currently believes
Where the business model breaks
Realistic downside revenue scenario
Cost & margin collapse risk
Risks the market is ignoring
Downside price target with math
What would make the bear case wrong
1
What is the market pricing in?
Before arguing the downside, understand what growth, margins, and outcomes the current share price already requires. That's what needs to be dismantled.
2
Where does the business model break?
Find the 2–3 most fragile assumptions. What has to go perfectly right for the bull case to work — and how realistic is that?
3
Revenue downside scenario
Realistic numbers — not worst-case fantasy. Consider: customer concentration, saturation, pricing pressure, and competition. Show the figures.
4
Margin & cash burn risk
If revenue disappoints, what happens to costs? Are they fixed or variable? How long does the balance sheet hold up? Estimate the runway.
5
The risk everyone is ignoring
1–2 underappreciated threats not in the consensus view. Regulatory shift, a competitor move, a technology disruption. Why is the market sleeping on it?
6
Downside price target + exit signal
Bear-case valuation with the multiple and revenue assumption shown. Percentage downside from current price. And the one thing that would make the bear case wrong.
