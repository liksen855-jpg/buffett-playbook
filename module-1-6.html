Asset Classes 101

A complete map of every major asset class — stocks, bonds, real estate, commodities, currencies, crypto, and ETFs. Their mechanics, their risk-return profiles, and how they interact with each other across market cycles.

65 min Video · Article · Quiz · Diagram
Section 01

Equities (Stocks)

Equities represent ownership in a business. A share of stock is a fractional claim on the company's assets, earnings, and future cash flows. Shareholders are residual claimants — they get what is left after everyone else (creditors, employees, tax authorities) has been paid.

This creates the equity risk premium: stocks are riskier than bonds (equity holders are last in the capital structure), so they must offer higher expected returns over the long run to attract capital. Historically, global equities have returned approximately 7–10% annually in real terms.

Section 02

Fixed Income (Bonds)

Bonds are debt instruments. When you buy a bond, you are lending money to the issuer (government or corporation) in exchange for regular interest payments (coupons) and the return of principal at maturity. Bond holders are senior to equity holders in the capital structure — they get paid first if the company fails.

The Key Relationship

Bond price and yield move inversely. When interest rates rise, existing bond prices fall (and vice versa). A bond's sensitivity to rate changes is measured by its duration — the weighted average time to receive cash flows. This is why the 2022 rate hike cycle produced the worst bond market in 100 years for long-duration holders.

Section 03

Real Estate

Real estate provides two return streams simultaneously: income (rent yield) and capital appreciation. It is a real asset — its value tends to move with inflation over the long run. This combination of yield and inflation protection makes it a standard institutional portfolio allocation.

REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts) allow investors to access real estate returns without buying physical property. They are required to distribute 90%+ of taxable income as dividends, creating high yield instruments with equity-like volatility.

The Malaysia Context

Malaysia's REIT sector (IGB REIT, Pavilion REIT, Sunway REIT) has historically offered 5–7% distribution yields with inflation protection from rental escalations. The sector's correlation with interest rates is the primary risk — rising rates compress REIT valuations by raising the discount rate on future distributions and competing with bond yields.

Section 04

Commodities

Commodities are raw materials — energy (oil, gas), metals (gold, silver, copper), and agricultural goods. They do not generate earnings or pay dividends; they derive value from supply-demand dynamics in physical markets.

Their primary investment function is twofold: inflation hedging (commodity prices often drive inflation) and portfolio diversification (low correlation with equities in most regimes).

Gold: The Special Case

Gold occupies a unique position in the asset class universe. It is neither a productive asset (like stocks) nor a yielding asset (like bonds). It is a monetary asset — a store of value with a 5,000-year track record. Its price is primarily driven by real interest rates (negative real rates are strongly bullish for gold) and central bank demand (especially from emerging market central banks seeking dollar diversification).

Section 05

ETFs & Index Funds

ETFs (Exchange Traded Funds) are pooled investment vehicles that trade on stock exchanges. They can track an index (S&P 500), a sector (technology), a commodity (gold), a bond market, or virtually any defined strategy. They have democratized institutional-grade diversification at near-zero cost.

The Cost Advantage

The Vanguard Total Stock Market ETF (VTI) charges 0.03% annually. The average actively managed US equity fund charges 0.7–1.5%. Over 30 years, this 1% annual fee difference compounds to roughly 26% of total portfolio value — a staggering amount of wealth quietly transferred to fund managers. Index funds win by default in efficient markets.

Section 06

Crypto: The Speculative Asset Class

Cryptocurrency is the newest and most speculative major asset class. Bitcoin has a fixed supply schedule (21 million coins maximum), making it a potential store of value and inflation hedge by design. Ethereum provides a programmable blockchain platform with genuine utility.

As an investment, crypto exhibits extreme volatility (Bitcoin has experienced multiple 70–90% drawdowns), very high correlation with speculative equities during risk-off periods, and significant regulatory uncertainty. It belongs at the speculative end of any portfolio — if at all — and only at allocations that would not impair the overall portfolio if lost entirely.

The Honest Assessment

Crypto is not an inflation hedge in the short to medium term — its correlation with the Nasdaq during the 2022 selloff demonstrated that it trades as a high-beta risk asset, not as digital gold. Its long-run store-of-value narrative may prove correct, but that thesis requires decades to validate. Position sizing accordingly.
