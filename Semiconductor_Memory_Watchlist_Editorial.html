Executive Summary

A trillion-dollar rally meets its first real pullback.

Semiconductor and memory stocks have sold off sharply over the past week, with the sector benchmark falling 12.9% over two sessions — the sharpest such decline since March 2025. The catalysts are a mix of profit-taking after an extraordinary first-half run, a class-action lawsuit alleging DRAM price coordination among Micron, Samsung, and SK Hynix, and renewed valuation concerns following a historic run in AI-linked equities.

Technical damage across the group is uneven. Applied Materials, KLA, Marvell, and Micron remain above their 50-day moving averages despite sharp single-day losses, suggesting the underlying uptrend is bent but not broken. Seagate, Lumentum, Coherent, and Applied Optoelectronics have already broken below their 50-day averages, with Applied Optoelectronics and Lumentum showing the deepest damage in the group covered below. Notably, no name in this report has reached classically oversold RSI territory (below 30) as of this writing.

The AI Memory Trade's Poster Child Meets Its First Real Test

Micron's fiscal Q3 revenue surged 346% year-over-year to $41.46 billion, and the company has become the clearest single expression of the AI memory supercycle — up over 200% year-to-date before this week's pullback. A fresh class-action lawsuit alleging DRAM price coordination with Samsung and SK Hynix has added a new source of uncertainty, but the technical picture has not cracked: price remains 14.5% above its 50-day moving average, and RSI at 48.5 shows no sign of exhaustion. The 50-day line near $852 is the level that matters from here.

A 292% Re-Rating Arrives at Its First Real Pivot Point

Western Digital's re-rating this year — as much as 292% year-to-date through late June — reflects a market recognizing that AI infrastructure needs bulk storage capacity, not just compute. That thesis now faces its sharpest test: price is sitting almost exactly on its 50-day moving average, the tightest margin of any name in this report. Fab Capex Broadens Beyond Leading-Edge Logic

Applied Materials sells the equipment fabs need regardless of who wins the memory pricing war — deposition, etch, and process tools purchased by TSMC, Samsung, Micron, and Intel alike. The Sharpest Single-Day Move Masks an Undamaged Trend

KLA holds a near-monopoly in wafer inspection and process control — a toll-booth position on fab capex that doesn't depend on any single product cycle. The Deepest Pullback Among Large-Cap Optical Names

Lumentum's optical transceivers wire together the GPU clusters inside AI data centers, making it one of the more direct plays on infrastructure buildout pace — and one of the hardest hit in this rout. Price sits 18.9% below its 50-day moving average, and RSI at 37.7 is the closest reading to oversold territory among the large-cap names, though still above the classic 30 threshold. More Diversified, Somewhat Less Damaged

Coherent competes with Lumentum in data-center optics but carries a broader book of business spanning industrial lasers and compound semiconductor materials — diversification that has translated into a shallower pullback. The stock sits 9.7% below its 50-day average versus Lumentum's 18.9%, with RSI at 41.2. Near-term support is more likely at recent consolidation zones than at the 200-day line, which remains a distant $237.

The Watchlist's Deepest Technical Damage Meets a Genuine Growth Story

Applied Optoelectronics is the smallest and highest-beta name in this report, and it is trading true to form — down 13% on the day and 28.3% below its 50-day average, the steepest technical damage of any stock covered here. That volatility cuts both ways: revenue has more than tripled since mid-2024 on hyperscaler transceiver demand, and consensus now models the company's first profitable quarter since 2023 for its August 6 report.

Custom Silicon's Bellwether Holds Its Ground

Marvell designs the custom AI accelerators and networking silicon that hyperscalers increasingly want built to their own specification — a fabless model, manufactured largely by TSMC, that sits at the center of the shift toward in-house AI chip design. Despite a sharp one-day move, the stock remains 9.7% above its 50-day average, a trend structure that has not yet broken.
