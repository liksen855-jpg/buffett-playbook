01
What just happened
SoFi dropped 12% in a single day after reporting earnings. But the earnings were actually great. So what's going on?

On April 29, 2026, SoFi reported its Q1 results. Revenue was up 41% from last year. Profit more than doubled. They added over a million new customers in just three months. By almost every measure it was a record quarter.

So why did the stock fall? Two reasons. First, management chose not to raise their full-year 2026 guidance despite the record quarter — investors were expecting an upgrade, and not getting one disappointed them. Second, one part of the business — their tech platform that processes payments for other fintech apps — lost a major client called Chime, which had already moved to a different provider. The market focused on those two negatives and ignored everything else.

The key point
The Chime situation was already known and announced months ago. The market reacted as if it was new information. That kind of overreaction is where opportunities tend to appear.

To make it even more interesting: right after the stock dropped, SoFi's own CEO went out and bought $498,000 worth of shares with his own personal money — not company grants, not options, his actual cash. That's a meaningful signal of internal confidence.

The really important thing to understand is that SoFi now has a full banking license. That might sound boring, but it's actually their biggest advantage. Here's why:

The bank charter advantage — explained simply
Before the banking license, SoFi had to borrow money from big banks at high rates to fund the loans they made. Now they use their customers' deposits instead — over $40 billion worth. Those deposits cost them significantly less in interest than borrowing from banks. The result: SoFi saves an estimated $622 million every year just in funding costs. That goes straight to the bottom line.

04
Is the stock cheap?
We ran multiple ways of estimating what SoFi is actually worth. Here's the plain-English version of each.

Method 1 — Comparing to similar companies. Affirm and Upstart are both fintech lenders. SoFi is growing faster than both and is actually profitable (they're not). Yet SoFi's stock is valued at a lower multiple of revenue than either of them. That gap is the core of the undervaluation argument.

Method 2 — Growth-adjusted price. There's a simple ratio called PEG that compares a stock's price to how fast earnings are growing. A fair value PEG is roughly 1.0. SoFi's is 0.71 — meaning you're paying 29% less per unit of growth than fair value. The average fintech trades at 1.02. Applying a fair PEG to SoFi's growth rate implies a price around $23.

Method 3 — Future cash flows. Project what SoFi will earn over the next 5–10 years, then discount those future dollars back to today's value. Assumes 24% annual revenue growth (management guided 30% just for 2026). Result: fair value around $25.78 in the base case.

Method 4 — Break up the business. Value each of the three business segments separately, add them up, subtract debt, divide by shares. Lending is valued like a profitable bank. Banking is valued like a high-growth fintech. Tech Platform is valued even after the Chime damage. Result: $17–$23 depending on how much synergy credit you give the three segments working together.

06
Why it gets stronger over time
SoFi has a self-reinforcing cycle built into the business. Understanding this is the key to the long-term thesis.

Here's the cycle in plain language:

1
New members join for one product
Usually a high-yield savings account or a loan. SoFi added 1.05 million new members in Q1 2026 alone.
2
They add more products over time
43% of new products opened in Q1 came from existing customers — up from 36% a year ago. Once someone is a member, SoFi is getting better at keeping them and selling them more.
3
Deposits pile up
More members with savings accounts means more deposits. SoFi now holds $40.2 billion. This is essentially free money compared to what they used to pay to borrow from Wall Street.
4
Cheaper money = fatter margins on loans
SoFi lends that cheap deposit money out at higher rates. The gap (called the net interest margin) is now 5.94%. That margin is expanding, not shrinking.
↻
Profits fund the next round of growth
Higher profits = more money to spend on acquiring new members, which starts the cycle again. And each cycle is cheaper than the last because existing members refer and cross-buy.
Why this matters for valuation
This cycle is getting stronger, not weaker. The percentage of new products coming from existing members went from 36% → 40% → 43% over the past year. As that number keeps climbing, the cost of growing the business keeps falling — which is why profit margins are expanding even as revenue grows fast.
07
What could go wrong
Being honest about the risks is as important as the upside case. Here are the real ones.
Genuine risks
Short-seller allegations. In March 2026, Muddy Waters Research published a report alleging accounting irregularities at SoFi, including $312M in alleged unrecorded debt. SoFi called the report "factually inaccurate" and threatened legal action. A securities fraud investigation was also opened by law firm Block & Leviton. These allegations are unresolved and create headline risk.
Recession hits borrowers hard. If unemployment spikes, more people miss loan payments. SoFi's borrowers are high income but not immune.
Tech platform loses more clients. If another big customer leaves Galileo, revenue estimates need to come down.
Guidance miss. Management said 30%+ revenue growth for 2026. If they cut that, the stock could fall further regardless of fundamentals.
Competition for deposits. If other banks offer higher savings rates, SoFi may have to raise theirs — shrinking the cost advantage.
What protects you
Massive capital buffer. 21.1% vs. 7% required minimum. Even a severe downturn wouldn't threaten the bank's solvency.
Prime borrowers. Average customer earns $154K with a 745 credit score. They default far less than average.
Bear case still beats current price. Even the worst modeled scenario ($17.05) is above today's $16.56.
CEO buying with personal money. Noto spent $498K of his own cash buying the dip right after earnings. That's real conviction.

The bottom line

SoFi reported its best quarter ever and the stock dropped 12%. The market is pricing it like a struggling bank. The numbers say it's a profitable fintech growing at 41% per year with $40 billion in cheap deposits and a CEO buying his own stock at the lows.

Every method used to estimate fair value — comparing to peers, projecting future profits, breaking the business into parts — points to a price between $23 and $26. The current price is $16.56. Even the most pessimistic scenario modeled ($17.05) is above where the stock is trading today.

That doesn't mean the stock goes up tomorrow. Markets can stay wrong for a long time. But the gap between what the business is actually worth and what you can buy it for right now is one of the clearest we've found in the fintech space.
