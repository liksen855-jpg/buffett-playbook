How Markets Work

The mechanical reality of exchanges, order books, and price discovery — plus the asymmetric frameworks the masses never learn: Soros's reflexivity, liquidity as the true risk, and how to read crowd positioning as a contrarian signal.

70 min Article · 2 Tools · 10-Q Quiz
Section 01

What Is a Stock Market?

A stock market is a marketplace where buyers and sellers exchange ownership stakes in publicly listed companies. When you buy a share, you are purchasing a fractional claim on the company's assets, earnings, and future cash flows. You become, legally and economically, a part-owner.

The market serves two distinct functions that are often confused:

  • Primary market: Companies raise capital by selling new shares to the public through an IPO. The company receives the proceeds. This is the capital formation function.
  • Secondary market: After the IPO, investors trade shares among themselves on exchanges. The company receives no proceeds. This is where virtually all daily trading occurs.
Key Concept

When you buy Apple stock on the NYSE, you are not giving Apple any money. You are buying shares from another investor who chose to sell. Apple's role ended at the IPO. The secondary market is simply investors continuously reassessing the value of already-issued shares — and disagreeing with each other enough to trade.

Section 02

Market Participants

Markets are not populated by a homogeneous mass of "investors." There are distinct participant types with different goals, time horizons, information advantages, and impact on price. Understanding who is on the other side of your trade matters more than most retail investors realise.

Market Participant Ecosystem
Asymmetric Edge

When you place a market order on a major US exchange, you are almost certainly trading against an HFT algorithm that knows the order book better than you do, faster than you can blink. This is not a conspiracy — it is the infrastructure of modern markets. The implication: for retail investors, speed is not your edge. Your edges are time horizon (you can hold for years; they cannot), position size (you can enter small positions that institutions cannot without moving the market), and patience. Never compete on their terms. Win on yours.

Section 03

Price Discovery & the Order Book

Prices are not assigned — they emerge. Price discovery is the process by which markets aggregate the opinions of all participants about what a security is worth, and express that consensus as a single number: the current price.

The mechanism is the order book — a real-time ledger of all outstanding buy (bid) and sell (ask) orders at every price level. The current market price is simply the intersection of the highest willing buyer and lowest willing seller.

What the Spread Tells You

A tight spread (e.g., $0.01 on Apple) signals high liquidity — many participants, efficient pricing, low cost of execution. A wide spread (e.g., $0.50 on a small-cap) signals low liquidity — your order will move the price against you. Always check the spread before entering any position. Wide spreads silently eat your returns.

Section 04

Order Types Every Investor Must Know

Your choice of order type is not a technicality — it directly affects execution price, risk, and strategy. These are the four order types that matter:

Order TypeExecutionPrice CertaintyBest For
Market OrderImmediate at best available priceNone — price can slipHigh-liquidity stocks, urgency
Limit OrderOnly at your specified price or betterFull — your price or no dealMost long-term investing
Stop OrderBecomes market order when price hits stopNone after triggerCutting losses, protecting gains
Stop-LimitBecomes limit order when price hits stopPrice certainty after triggerPrecision risk management
Practical Insight

For most long-term investors, limit orders are superior to market orders in virtually all situations. A market order for an illiquid stock during volatile conditions can execute significantly worse than the displayed price — this is called slippage. Always use limit orders unless speed of execution is genuinely critical. The extra 5 minutes it takes to get a fill is worth far less than the 1–3% you might otherwise surrender to slippage.

Section 05

Market Efficiency & What It Means for You

The Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH) proposes that stock prices at all times reflect all available information. In its strong form, this would mean no investor can consistently beat the market — any advantage would be instantly arbitraged away.

The evidence is mixed and nuanced. Markets are mostly efficient for widely-followed, liquid, large-cap securities. But inefficiencies persist at the edges.

Three Forms of Market Efficiency

The practical implication: beating the market in large-cap, well-covered US stocks is extraordinarily difficult because thousands of analysts are pricing every data point in real time. Beating the market in less-efficient areas — emerging markets, microcaps, event-driven situations, complex corporate structures — is possible, but requires genuine informational or analytical edge, not just effort.

Asymmetric Edge — Where Inefficiency Lives

The market is most efficient where the most eyeballs are watching. It is least efficient where: (1) securities are too small for institutions to participate (sub-$300M market cap), (2) there is a structural reason why sophisticated investors can't or won't look (regulatory restrictions, stigma, complexity), (3) the time horizon required is longer than institutional mandates allow (multi-year thesis), (4) it's a market where language or geography creates barriers (local EM markets, foreign small-caps). These four inefficiency pockets are where Druckenmiller, Soros, and every other outperformer built their careers. The question isn't "can you beat Apple?" — it's "where are the blind spots in the system?"

Section 06

Bull Markets, Bear Markets & Market Cycles

Markets move in cycles. Understanding cycle vocabulary and mechanics prevents the most common and costly mistake in investing: panic selling at lows and exuberant buying at highs.

  • Bull market: Sustained price rise of 20%+ from recent lows. Rising confidence, expanding earnings expectations, often leverage. Historically lasts 3–5 years on average.
  • Bear market: Sustained decline of 20%+ from recent highs. Falling earnings expectations, risk aversion, forced selling. Average decline: −35%, average duration: 9–14 months.
  • Correction: A 10–20% pullback within a larger trend. Often sudden, often reversible within weeks. The most common buying opportunity most investors talk themselves out of.
  • Crash: Rapid decline of 20%+ in days or weeks. Almost always driven by liquidity events and margin calls — not fundamental deterioration. The 1987 crash reversed 100% within 2 years.
Historical Context

The S&P 500 has experienced a bear market (–20%+) approximately every 3.5 years on average. It has recovered and made new all-time highs every single time. The investor who understood this and did not sell in 2009, 2020, or 2022 made extraordinary returns. The one who panicked made losses permanent. Bear markets are not threats to the long-term investor — they are the mechanism that generates long-term equity returns.

Section 07 · Asymmetric Framework

Soros's Reflexivity: The Operating System of Markets

The most important concept about markets that almost no standard investing course teaches. George Soros built the most successful macro hedge fund in history on one core insight: markets do not simply reflect reality — they create it.

The standard model of markets assumes that prices reflect underlying fundamental value, and that when prices deviate from fundamentals, they eventually correct back. Reflexivity challenges this entirely. It says the relationship runs in both directions: fundamentals influence prices, but prices also influence fundamentals.

"
Markets are constantly in a state of uncertainty and flux, and money is made by discounting the obvious and betting on the unexpected. The prevailing wisdom is usually wrong at turning points.
— George Soros, The Alchemy of Finance

The Reflexive Feedback Loop

Here is a concrete example of reflexivity in action. A stock begins to rise on optimistic expectations. As it rises:

  • The company's cost of capital falls — it can now issue equity cheaply and raise cash for acquisitions or growth investment.
  • Management credibility increases — a rising stock price attracts better talent, better deal terms, more press coverage.
  • The business improves — the higher stock price literally made the company's fundamentals better than they were before the rise.
  • The narrative self-reinforces — analysts upgrade, momentum investors pile in, index funds increase their weighting. More buying begets more buying.

The critical insight: the price rise created the fundamental improvement that now justifies the higher price. This is not a mispricing that will correct — it is a new reality that the market created. And it can continue far longer, and reach far higher, than any "fair value" model would predict.

Asymmetric Edge — How to Trade Reflexivity

On the way up: Reflexive trends have more momentum than fundamentals suggest. Don't fight the trend simply because valuation looks stretched. The feedback loop is real, and it can persist for years. Soros rode UK real estate reflexivity through the late 1980s, Druckenmiller rode US tech reflexivity through the 1990s. The question isn't "is it overvalued?" — it's "is the feedback loop intact or breaking?"

On the way down: Reflexive collapses are equally violent and self-reinforcing. A falling stock price raises the cost of capital, hurts management credibility, triggers analyst downgrades, forces index rebalancing, hits management stock options, and can trigger covenant breaches on debt — all of which make the underlying business worse, justifying the lower price. This is why value traps exist. The stock looks cheap at −40%, gets worse at −60%, and is still getting worse at −80%. Reflexivity is working in reverse.

The edge: Identify where the feedback loop is about to reverse. The inflection from reinforcing to reversing is the highest-conviction entry point in all of markets. It requires watching fundamentals and the narrative simultaneously — the moment the fundamental improvement can no longer justify the price, or the moment a collapsing business stabilises, is where the asymmetric trade lives.

Section 08 · Asymmetric Framework

Liquidity: The Real Risk That Causes Every Crash

Most investing courses teach risk as volatility — the standard deviation of returns. This is technically rigorous and completely wrong for how markets actually blow up. The real risk is liquidity risk: the risk that when you want to sell, there is no buyer.

Every major market crash in history — 1929, 1987, 1998, 2008, March 2020 — was not primarily a story of fundamentals deteriorating. It was a story of liquidity evaporating. The bid disappeared. And when the bid disappears, price discovery stops working and assets fall to whatever level can attract a buyer — which can be far, far below intrinsic value.

The Liquidity Cascade — How Crashes Actually Happen
01
Initial Trigger
A forced seller appears — a fund redemption, a margin call, a regulatory action, a surprise earnings miss. They need to sell regardless of price.
LOCALISED
02
Price Decline Triggers More Margin Calls
The forced sale moves prices down. Other leveraged investors now have margin calls of their own. They sell whatever is liquid — often assets unrelated to the original trigger.
SPREADING
03
Market Makers Widen Spreads or Step Back
HFT algorithms detect volatility and widen bid-ask spreads dramatically. Some pull their quotes entirely. The "cost" of selling spikes. Buyers demand a much larger discount to take risk.
ACCELERATING
04
Correlation Goes to 1
In a liquidity crisis, diversification stops working. Everything sells together — stocks, bonds, commodities, crypto, EM currencies. Investors don't sell what they want to sell; they sell what they can sell.
CRISIS
05
Resolution: The Patient Buyer Wins
The cascade stops when buyers with no leverage and no forced selling obligations step in. Those who preserved dry powder — cash, low debt — buy assets at generational discounts from panicked forced sellers.
OPPORTUNITY
Asymmetric Edge — The Liquidity Framework

Before a position: Ask — if I need to exit this, how large is the market? How wide is the spread? What happens to my exit price in a risk-off environment? A position that looks attractively priced but has thin liquidity can destroy more capital in an exit than the investment ever would have made.

Portfolio construction: Always hold a liquidity reserve. The exact amount is a personal choice, but the purpose is fixed: it is not a return-seeking asset. It is ammunition for the moments when everyone else is forced to sell. Druckenmiller has described his 1992 sterling trade and his 2008 positioning as both being built on holding cash until the opportunity became obvious — then concentrating aggressively. You cannot concentrate when you have no dry powder.

Crisis recognition: Watch credit spreads (the yield difference between corporate bonds and government bonds). When spreads widen sharply, it is the bond market pricing in liquidity stress before the equity market has caught up. This is the single most reliable early warning system available, and most retail investors never look at it.

Section 09 · Asymmetric Framework

Crowd Positioning: Reading Consensus as a Contrarian Signal

When everyone who is going to buy has bought, there are no buyers left — only sellers. This sounds tautological, but it is the most powerful and most ignored principle in markets.

Price is not just a reflection of what people believe about fundamentals. It is also a reflection of what position they have already taken. A stock with universally bullish sentiment, record institutional ownership, and maximum analyst coverage may well have excellent fundamentals — but if there is no incremental buyer, it cannot go up.

"
When you find yourself agreeing with the consensus, you should be very worried. The market has already discounted what everyone agrees on.
— Stanley Druckenmiller

The Positioning Data That Matters

These are the institutional-grade sentiment and positioning indicators that professional macro investors monitor. Most retail investors are unaware they exist:

Indicator · 01
CFTC Commitment of Traders (COT)
Published weekly. Shows net positioning of commercial hedgers, large speculators, and small speculators across futures markets — currencies, commodities, equity indices. When speculators are at historical extremes of long or short, the contrarian trade is statistically strong.
CONTRARIAN SIGNAL
Indicator · 02
BofA Fund Manager Survey
Monthly survey of global fund managers. Tracks: cash levels, equity overweight/underweight, sector positioning, and "most crowded trades." When cash is at record highs (fear) or record lows (greed), historically it has been time to do the opposite.
MONTHLY DATA
Indicator · 03
Put/Call Ratio
Ratio of put options (bets on decline) to call options (bets on rise). Very high reading = extreme fear = often a near-term bottom. Very low reading = extreme complacency = often a near-term top. Most powerful as a contrarian indicator at extremes, not as a trend signal.
VOLATILITY SIGNAL
Indicator · 04
Short Interest
Percentage of a stock's float that is sold short. Very high short interest can signal either: a legitimate warning (smart money sees a problem) or a short squeeze opportunity if the thesis is wrong. The distinction between the two requires fundamental analysis, not just the data.
CONTEXT-DEPENDENT
Indicator · 05
AAII Sentiment Survey
Weekly survey of individual investors. Bull/Bear ratio is a reliable contrarian indicator at extremes. When 60%+ bears: historically a near-term bottom in 80% of cases. When 60%+ bulls: historically preceded underperformance in subsequent 6 months.
CONTRARIAN SIGNAL
Indicator · 06
Credit Spreads (IG & HY)
The spread between investment-grade or high-yield corporate bond yields and equivalent Treasury yields. Widening spreads = credit stress = leading indicator for equity volatility. Narrowing spreads = risk appetite returning. The bond market is smarter than the stock market and always moves first.
EARLY WARNING
Asymmetric Edge — The Contrarian Framework

Positioning data is not a trading signal. It is a risk-management input. When all of the indicators above simultaneously flash extreme bullish positioning — record low cash, record high equity exposure, record high call/put ratio, max analyst optimism — it does not mean sell everything immediately. It means: (1) you have no margin of safety, (2) the upside is limited because all potential buyers are already long, and (3) any negative surprise will produce an outsized reaction because there are no incremental buyers to absorb selling pressure.

Conversely, when all indicators flash extreme bearish positioning, the fundamental backdrop matters less. Even mediocre news can trigger sharp rallies simply because shorts need to cover and underinvested institutions need to get back to benchmark weight. The fuel for the next rally is already sitting in cash and short positions. Ackman's maxim applies here exactly: "The consensus is wrong at turning points."
